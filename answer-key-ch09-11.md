## Chapter 9 Answers: Thermodynamic Principles II

### Chapter 9 — Problem 1

**(a)** In a spontaneous redox reaction, electrons flow from the compound with the more negative (lower) reduction potential to the compound with the more positive (higher) reduction potential. Compound A has E°′ = −0.19 V; Compound B has E°′ = +0.12 V. Electrons flow from A to B: Compound A (reduced form) is oxidized, and Compound B (oxidized form) is reduced.

**(b)** ΔE°′ = E°′(acceptor) − E°′(donor) = +0.12 − (−0.19) = +0.31 V

$$\Delta G^{\circ'} = -nF\Delta E^{\circ'} = -(2)(96.5\;\text{kJ/V·mol})(+0.31\;\text{V}) = -59.8\;\text{kJ/mol}$$

The reaction is spontaneous (ΔG°′ < 0) in the direction written in part (a).

**(c)** Compound B (reduced) donates electrons to $\text{O}_2$ (E°′ = +0.82 V):

ΔE°′ = +0.82 − (+0.12) = +0.70 V

$$\Delta G^{\circ'} = -(2)(96.5)(+0.70) = -135.1\;\text{kJ/mol}$$

For $\text{NADH}$-to-oxygen transfer: ΔE°′ = +0.82 − (−0.32) = +1.14 V, giving ΔG°′ = −(2)(96.5)(1.14) = −220.1 kJ/mol. The Compound B-to-$\text{O}_2$ transfer releases less energy (−135.1 kJ/mol) than $\text{NADH}$-to-$\text{O}_2$ (−220.1 kJ/mol) because Compound B (reduced) has a higher reduction potential than $\text{NADH}$, meaning its electrons are at a lower energy state (less reducing power). The drop in potential from Compound B to $\text{O}_2$ is smaller than the drop from $\text{NADH}$ to $\text{O}_2$, so less free energy is released.

### Chapter 9 — Problem 2

For electron transfer to be spontaneous, electrons must flow from the substrate (more negative E°′) to the carrier (more positive E°′). The substrate half-reaction must have a more negative E°′ than the carrier to which it donates.

**(a)** Substrate E°′ = −0.18 V. $\text{NAD}^+$/$\text{NADH}$ has E°′ = −0.32 V. Since −0.18 V is more positive than −0.32 V, the substrate is a weaker reducing agent than $\text{NADH}$; electrons cannot spontaneously flow from the substrate to $\text{NAD}^+$ (the transfer would be thermodynamically unfavorable: ΔE°′ = −0.32 − (−0.18) = −0.14 V, ΔG°′ > 0). FAD/$\text{FADH}_2$ has E°′ ≈ 0 V. Here ΔE°′ = 0 − (−0.18) = +0.18 V > 0, so electron transfer from this substrate to FAD is spontaneous. FAD is the correct carrier.

**(b)** Substrate E°′ = +0.05 V. FAD has E°′ ≈ 0 V. ΔE°′ = 0 − (+0.05) = −0.05 V < 0; transfer to FAD is not spontaneous. $\text{NAD}^+$ has E°′ = −0.32 V; ΔE°′ = −0.32 − (+0.05) = −0.37 V < 0; transfer to $\text{NAD}^+$ is even less favorable. Neither canonical carrier can accept electrons from a substrate this oxidized under standard conditions. In practice, substrates with E°′ more positive than ~0 V cannot be oxidized by either $\text{NAD}^+$ or FAD. (This is why, for example, the fumarate/succinate couple at +0.03 V uses a specially tuned enzyme-bound FAD with a shifted E°′ near 0 V, making the transfer borderline feasible; a freely diffusing FAD cannot do it, which is why the E3 subunit's FAD in PDH has a shifted E°′ as well.)

### Chapter 9 — Problem 4

**(a)** Succinate oxidized by $\text{NAD}^+$: electrons flow from succinate (E°′(fumarate/succinate) = +0.03 V) to $\text{NAD}^+$ (E°′($\text{NAD}^+$/$\text{NADH}$) = −0.32 V).

ΔE°′ = E°′(acceptor) − E°′(donor) = −0.32 − (+0.03) = −0.35 V

$$\Delta G^{\circ'} = -(2)(96.5)(-0.35) = +67.6\;\text{kJ/mol}$$

Highly unfavorable. The reaction as written is non-spontaneous; it proceeds only if the bacterial enzyme couples it to a strongly exergonic process.

**(b)** Mammalian cells use FAD for succinate oxidation because FAD (E°′ ≈ 0 V) is close in potential to the fumarate/succinate couple (+0.03 V). ΔE°′ ≈ −0.03 V, and under cellular conditions (continuous removal of fumarate by the next enzyme) the reaction is pulled forward. $\text{NAD}^+$ at −0.32 V cannot accept electrons from a substrate at +0.03 V without external energy input. The constraint: the electron carrier must have an E°′ equal to or more positive than the donor substrate's E°′.

**(c)** If $\text{NADH}$ were produced instead of $\text{FADH}_2$, it would enter Complex I of the electron transport chain, yielding approximately 2.5 $\text{ATP}$ equivalents, versus 1.5 $\text{ATP}$ for $\text{FADH}_2$ entering at Complex II. Replacing $\text{FADH}_2$ with $\text{NADH}$ would nominally be a +1.0 $\text{ATP}$ benefit per succinate oxidized. However, this is a theoretical calculation on a thermodynamically forbidden reaction. The cell uses FAD not by choice but by necessity; the cost of obtaining only 1.5 $\text{ATP}$ at this step is the price of being able to oxidize succinate at all given the available reduction potential.

### Chapter 9 — Problem 5

One example from outside glycolysis and the TCA cycle for each of the six reaction types covered in the chapter:

1. **Oxidation-reduction**: The oxidation of $\text{NADH}$ by Complex I of the electron transport chain. Electrons are transferred from $\text{NADH}$ to ubiquinone, regenerating $\text{NAD}^+$ and producing ubiquinol, driving proton pumping across the inner mitochondrial membrane.

2. **Ligation (requiring $\text{ATP}$)**: Glutamine synthetase catalyzes the $\text{ATP}$-dependent condensation of glutamate and ammonia to form glutamine. $\text{ATP}$ is hydrolyzed to $\text{ADP}$ + $P_i$, driving this otherwise unfavorable amination.

3. **Isomerization**: Glucose-6-phosphate isomerase converts glucose-6-phosphate to fructose-6-phosphate at the entry of glycolysis — but staying outside glycolysis, phosphoglucose isomerase in the pentose phosphate pathway similarly rearranges sugar phosphates (e.g., ribulose-5-phosphate to ribose-5-phosphate by phosphopentose isomerase).

4. **Group transfer (not involving $\text{ATP}$)**: Aminotransferases (transaminases) transfer an amino group from an amino acid (e.g., alanine) to α-ketoglutarate, producing pyruvate and glutamate. The amino group is the transferred moiety; PLP is the cofactor.

5. **Hydrolysis**: Intestinal sucrase cleaves sucrose into glucose and fructose by hydrolyzing the α(1→2β) glycosidic bond, using water as the nucleophile. No energy input required; the reaction is exergonic.

6. **Addition or removal across a double bond (dehydration/hydration)**: Fumarase adds water across the double bond of fumarate to form malate (TCA cycle, excluded) — an equivalent outside example: enoyl-ACP reductase in fatty acid synthesis reduces the double bond of trans-$\Delta^2$-enoyl-ACP to form a saturated acyl-ACP (addition of $\text{H}_2$ across the double bond, using N$\text{ADP}$H).

### Chapter 9 — Problem 6

**(a)** The actual ΔG under cellular conditions:

$$\Delta G = \Delta G^{\circ'} + RT\ln Q = \Delta G^{\circ'} + RT\ln\frac{[Y]}{[X]}$$

$$\Delta G = +8.1 + (8.314 \times 10^{-3}\;\text{kJ/mol·K})(310\;\text{K})\ln\!\left(\frac{0.01}{0.4}\right)$$

$$= +8.1 + (2.578)(−3.689) = +8.1 − 9.51 = -1.41\;\text{kJ/mol}$$

**(b)** Yes, the reaction is spontaneous under these cellular conditions. ΔG = −1.41 kJ/mol < 0. Although ΔG°′ is positive (the reaction is unfavorable at standard concentrations of 1 M), the cell maintains [Y] far below [X] (a 40:1 ratio of substrate to product), shifting the equilibrium to favor the forward direction.

**(c)** The student confuses ΔG°′ with ΔG. ΔG°′ describes thermodynamics only at standard conditions (all reactants and products at 1 M, 25°C, pH 7). Actual ΔG includes a concentration term (RT ln Q) that can make any reaction spontaneous or non-spontaneous depending on how far the actual concentrations are from equilibrium. If the cell keeps [Y] very low relative to [X] — for example by having a downstream enzyme rapidly consume Y — the mass action term becomes strongly negative and can overwhelm a positive ΔG°′. Many metabolic reactions with positive ΔG°′ proceed forward in cells because of favorable concentration ratios maintained by coupled downstream steps.

**(d)** With [Y] = 2.0 mM and [X] = 0.4 mM:

$$\Delta G = +8.1 + (2.578)\ln\!\left(\frac{2.0}{0.4}\right) = +8.1 + (2.578)(1.609) = +8.1 + 4.15 = +12.25\;\text{kJ/mol}$$

ΔG is now positive. The reaction is no longer spontaneous; in fact it runs backward. With product Y accumulating (because the downstream enzyme is blocked), Q exceeds 1, the concentration term becomes positive and adds to the already positive ΔG°′. Flux through the X → Y step would stop and potentially reverse, causing Y to flow back to X. This illustrates how inhibiting one enzyme propagates backward through a pathway, backing up metabolites.

### Chapter 9 — Problem 7

**(a)** Thioester hydrolysis has ΔG°′ ≈ −31 kJ/mol, essentially equal to $\text{ATP}$ hydrolysis (−30.5 kJ/mol). This means a thioester bond stores approximately as much free energy as a phosphoanhydride bond in $\text{ATP}$. Thioesters can therefore drive reactions that are thermodynamically coupled to their hydrolysis: condensation reactions, acyl transfer reactions, and other biosynthetic steps that would otherwise be thermodynamically unfavorable. Acetyl-CoA, for example, can donate its acetyl group to acceptors in reactions that are thermodynamically driven by the energy released when the thioester is cleaved.

**(b)** Citrate synthase condenses acetyl-CoA with oxaloacetate to form citryl-CoA, which is then hydrolyzed to citrate and free CoA in the same active site. The CoA released carries nothing; the thioester bond is broken, releasing ~31 kJ/mol. This large release of free energy makes the overall condensation reaction highly exergonic (ΔG°′ ≈ −32 kJ/mol for the overall reaction) without any $\text{ATP}$ input. The energy comes from the thioester bond itself: breaking it provides the thermodynamic driving force for forming the new C-C bond between acetyl and oxaloacetate. $\text{ATP}$ is not needed because the thioester already carries sufficient energy.

**(c)** Fatty acid activation: $\text{fatty acid} + \text{CoA} + \text{ATP} \rightarrow \text{fatty acyl-CoA} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i$. Pyrophosphate ($\text{PP}_i$) is immediately hydrolyzed by pyrophosphatase: $\text{PP}_i \rightarrow 2P_i$ (ΔG°′ ≈ −19 kJ/mol). In total, the reaction consumes the equivalent of 2 $\text{ATP}$: the $\text{ATP} \rightarrow \text{AMP}$ step releases 2 high-energy phosphate bonds (the α-β bond cleaved to give AMP + $\text{PP}_i$ is equivalent to ~2 × the $\text{ATP} \rightarrow \text{ADP}$ hydrolysis energy), plus the pyrophosphatase step provides an additional thermodynamic pull. This is effectively 2 $\text{ATP}$ equivalents consumed. Using the AMP-producing cleavage (rather than $\text{ADP}$ + $P_i$) provides greater thermodynamic driving force because the pyrophosphatase step is highly exergonic and irreversible, pulling the entire activation forward. A reaction that produced $\text{ADP}$ + $P_i$ would release only one high-energy bond and would be readily reversible; the two-step mechanism produces a near-irreversible activation by coupling two exergonic hydrolysis steps.

### Chapter 9 — Problem 8

**(a)** Complex I oxidizes $\text{NADH}$ to $\text{NAD}^+$ and transfers electrons to ubiquinone. When Complex I is inhibited, $\text{NADH}$ oxidation is slowed. $\text{NADH}$ accumulates in the mitochondrial matrix and the $\text{NADH}$/$\text{NAD}^+$ ratio rises.

**(b)** Three metabolic pathways that require $\text{NAD}^+$:

1. **Glycolysis**: GAPDH (step 6) oxidizes glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate to 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate, reducing $\text{NAD}^+$ to $\text{NADH}$. When $\text{NAD}^+$ is scarce, GAPDH stalls, blocking glycolytic flux downstream of step 5.

2. **Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex (PDH)**: The E3 subunit reoxidizes $\text{FADH}_2$ using $\text{NAD}^+$ as the terminal acceptor, regenerating $\text{NAD}^+$ for continued PDH activity. Elevated $\text{NADH}$ inhibits PDH, reducing acetyl-CoA production and slowing TCA cycle entry.

3. **TCA cycle**: Three dehydrogenation steps (isocitrate dehydrogenase, α-KG dehydrogenase, malate dehydrogenase) reduce $\text{NAD}^+$ to $\text{NADH}$. High $\text{NADH}$/$\text{NAD}^+$ directly inhibits all three enzymes via product inhibition, slowing the entire cycle.

**(c)** At therapeutic metformin doses, Complex I is only mildly inhibited; $\text{NADH}$/$\text{NAD}^+$ rises modestly, AMPK is activated (sensing elevated AMP from reduced $\text{ATP}$ synthesis), and glycolysis is suppressed via reduced gluconeogenesis. At overdose, severe Complex I inhibition dramatically raises $\text{NADH}$/$\text{NAD}^+$. Glycolysis stalls at GAPDH because $\text{NAD}^+$ is depleted. To regenerate $\text{NAD}^+$ and maintain glycolytic $\text{ATP}$ production, LDH reduces pyruvate to lactate (lactate dehydrogenase: $\text{pyruvate} + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^+ \rightarrow \text{lactate} + \text{NAD}^+$). Lactate is exported from the cell. Blood lactate rises to toxic levels, consuming bicarbonate buffer and causing metabolic acidosis.

## Chapter 10 Answers: Glycolysis and Gluconeogenesis

### Chapter 10 — Problem 1

**(a)** Net reaction for the payoff phase ($2\ \text{G3P} \rightarrow 2\ \text{pyruvate}$):

$$2\;\text{G3P} + 4\;\text{ADP} + 4\;\text{P}_i + 2\;\text{NAD}^+ \rightarrow 2\;\text{pyruvate} + 4\;\text{ATP} + 2\;\text{NADH} + 2\;\text{H}^+ + 2\;\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

**(b)** The payoff phase yields 4 $\text{ATP}$ rather than 2 because each of the two G3P molecules independently undergoes substrate-level phosphorylation twice: once at step 7 (phosphoglycerate kinase: $\text{1,3-BPG} \rightarrow \text{3-PG} + \text{ATP}$) and once at step 10 (pyruvate kinase: $\text{PEP} \rightarrow \text{pyruvate} + \text{ATP}$). With 2 G3P molecules entering, each yielding 2 $\text{ATP}$, the total is 4 $\text{ATP}$. The doubling was established in the investment phase: one glucose generates two triose phosphates (DHAP and G3P), and once TPI interconverts DHAP to G3P, both three-carbon units proceed through the payoff phase independently.

**(c)** Steps 6 and 7 are tightly coupled because their products and substrates are directly linked: step 6 (GAPDH) produces 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate (1,3-BPG), which is immediately consumed by step 7 (phosphoglycerate kinase, PGK). 1,3-BPG is a high-energy acyl phosphate (ΔG°′ for hydrolysis ≈ −49 kJ/mol); it accumulates only transiently before PGK cleaves it to drive $\text{ATP}$ synthesis. PGK's large negative ΔG°′ (highly exergonic $\text{ATP}$ formation from 1,3-BPG) keeps [1,3-BPG] very low, which continuously pulls step 6 forward by mass action even though GAPDH operates near equilibrium. Coupling works here not by direct allostery but by maintaining a steep concentration gradient for the shared intermediate: step 7's thermodynamic appetite for 1,3-BPG prevents its buildup, ensuring step 6 never reaches its own equilibrium.

### Chapter 10 — Problem 2

**(a)** Without TPI, DHAP cannot be converted to G3P. Starting from one glucose:

Investment phase: Glucose → glucose-6-phosphate → fructose-6-phosphate → fructose-1,6-bisphosphate → DHAP + G3P. Cost: 2 $\text{ATP}$ (hexokinase + PFK-1). One G3P is produced directly; the DHAP cannot be isomerized and is a dead end.

Payoff phase: Only the one G3P proceeds through the payoff phase, generating 2 $\text{ATP}$ and 1 $\text{NADH}$.

Net $\text{ATP}$: −2 (investment) + 2 (one G3P through payoff) = 0 $\text{ATP}$ net. $\text{NADH}$ yield: 1 $\text{NADH}$ per glucose. (Compared to wild type: net +2 $\text{ATP}$ and 2 $\text{NADH}$ per glucose.)

**(b)** TPI operates at the diffusion limit ($k_\text{cat}/K_m \approx 10^8$–$10^9\ \text{M}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1}$); it processes substrate as fast as it diffuses to the active site. An enzyme operating at the diffusion limit contributes no kinetic bottleneck; the pathway runs at the maximum rate set by substrate supply. When TPI is absent, the cell uses only half its triose phosphates, losing 50% of net $\text{ATP}$ yield. Losing an enzyme that was a kinetic bottleneck would slow one step but the flux loss is limited. Losing TPI eliminates the ability to use the other half of each glucose molecule entirely — a categorical metabolic loss rather than a rate reduction.

**(c)** The TPI equilibrium strongly favors DHAP (97:3), meaning the equilibrium G3P concentration is very low. In wild-type cells, G3P is continuously removed by GAPDH (step 6), which keeps [G3P] below even this already-low equilibrium level. By Le Chatelier's principle, continuous product removal pulls the TPI reaction forward despite the unfavorable equilibrium. G3P is the only substrate that can enter the payoff phase; its removal by GAPDH provides the thermodynamic pull that drives the 97:3-disfavored equilibrium in the productive direction. Without a downstream enzyme to consume G3P, the equilibrium would trap the flux at step 5.

### Chapter 10 — Problem 3

**(a)** $\text{ATP}$ at the active site (catalytic site) binds the substrate-binding pocket and donates its γ-phosphate to fructose-6-phosphate, acting as the phosphoryl donor in the reaction. This is the catalytic role; $\text{ATP}$'s binding affinity at the active site is relatively low (Km is in the millimolar range), appropriate for normal intracellular $\text{ATP}$ concentrations. The allosteric (regulatory) site is a distinct, physically separate pocket on the enzyme where $\text{ATP}$ binds with higher affinity. When $\text{ATP}$ occupies the allosteric site, it stabilizes the T (tensed, low-activity) conformational state of the enzyme, reducing the affinity of the active site for the other substrate, fructose-6-phosphate, and decreasing catalytic rate. The two sites are not in competition because they are different binding pockets with different affinities and different structural consequences. There is no paradox: the active site and the allosteric site happen to bind the same molecule ($\text{ATP}$), but they read it as different signals. At high $\text{ATP}$ concentrations, the allosteric site is saturated and glycolysis slows, which is exactly the appropriate cellular response to energy surplus.

**(b)** High cytoplasmic citrate is a signal that the TCA cycle is running faster than the cell can consume its products, meaning the cell has more than enough biosynthetic and energetic precursors. Citrate is the first product of the TCA cycle and is exported from the mitochondria into the cytoplasm when it accumulates; its cytoplasmic concentration rises when oxaloacetate and acetyl-CoA are both plentiful and the cycle is not limiting. Slowing glycolysis in response to high citrate prevents the cell from pushing more pyruvate and acetyl-CoA into pathways that are already saturated. It is a negative feedback signal from downstream to upstream: "the pipeline is full, stop feeding more substrate in." This is coherent because generating more acetyl-CoA when the TCA cycle is already backed up would only convert more glucose into wasted or incompletely oxidized intermediates, and would unnecessarily deplete the glucose supply.

**(c)** F-1,6-BP is a glycolytic intermediate: it is made by PFK-1 and cleaved by aldolase into the two triose phosphates. It accumulates when glycolysis is proceeding and participates directly in the metabolic flux. Its role as an allosteric activator of pyruvate kinase (PK) is a feedforward mechanism: high F-1,6-BP signals that PFK-1 has committed glucose molecules to glycolysis, so it makes sense to activate the downstream terminal enzyme PK to ensure the product (pyruvate) is generated proportionally. F-2,6-BP is not a glycolytic intermediate at all: it is synthesized and degraded by a bifunctional enzyme (PFK-2/FBPase-2) whose activity is regulated by hormonal signals. F-2,6-BP does not carry flux through glycolysis; it acts purely as an allosteric activator of PFK-1, potently increasing PFK-1 activity by shifting the enzyme into the R state and antagonizing $\text{ATP}$ inhibition. F-1,6-BP acts at pyruvate kinase (downstream of PFK-1) as a feedforward activator; F-2,6-BP acts at PFK-1 itself as a positive allosteric activator. The two molecules have the same atoms in different positions (F-2,6-BP has a phosphate at C2 instead of C1) and they act at different enzymes with entirely different regulatory logics.

### Chapter 10 — Problem 4

**(a)** Aldolase B, the hepatic isoform, normally cleaves fructose-1-phosphate into dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) and glyceraldehyde. In hereditary fructose intolerance, aldolase B is absent or nonfunctional. Dietary fructose enters the hepatocyte and is phosphorylated at C1 by fructokinase to fructose-1-phosphate (F-1-P). With no aldolase B to cleave it, F-1-P accumulates in the hepatocyte. This is the metabolite that accumulates and is responsible for the acute toxicity: it is not gluconeogenic, it traps inorganic phosphate (which cannot be regenerated once committed to F-1-P), and the accumulation becomes cytotoxic at high concentrations.

**(b)** Inorganic phosphate ($P_i$) has two essential roles being disrupted simultaneously. First, GAPDH (glycolytic step 6) uses $P_i$ as a substrate to phosphorylate glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate, forming 1,3-BPG. Without adequate $P_i$, GAPDH cannot proceed, and the payoff phase of glycolysis stalls. Second, and more acutely important, mitochondrial $\text{ATP}$ synthesis by the $\text{F}_1\text{F}_0$-ATPase also requires $P_i$ as a substrate: $\text{ATP}$ is made from $\text{ADP}$ + $P_i$ by oxidative phosphorylation. When $P_i$ is sequestered into accumulating F-1-P (which cannot be metabolized), the mitochondrial matrix is depleted of the inorganic phosphate needed for $\text{ATP}$ synthase to function. The result is rapid, severe hepatic $\text{ATP}$ depletion even when the electron transport chain and substrate oxidation are intact. Cells that are energetically starved, especially hepatocytes which are highly metabolically active and reliant on mitochondrial $\text{ATP}$, undergo injury and death.

**(c)** In the liver, glucose is phosphorylated by glucokinase (hexokinase IV) to glucose-6-phosphate, which enters glycolysis and gluconeogenesis only through the regulated PFK-1 step. Glucokinase has a high Km for glucose (acts as a glucose sensor) and is subject to normal regulatory control; glucose-6-phosphate can also be stored as glycogen. Fructose metabolism, in contrast, enters the pathway via fructokinase, which produces fructose-1-phosphate. Critically, fructokinase bypasses the PFK-1 regulatory checkpoint entirely: PFK-1 is the primary rate-limiting valve of glycolysis in the liver, allosterically responsive to $\text{ATP}$, AMP, citrate, and F-2,6-BP. Fructose sidesteps this checkpoint and commits carbon to the downstream triose phosphate pool without any regulatory oversight. When aldolase B is absent, fructose is therefore channeled into a metabolic trap (F-1-P accumulation) with no PFK-1 to slow the influx. Glucose never reaches this trap because its metabolism is gated at PFK-1, which limits flux and prevents the kind of uncontrolled carbon flooding that fructose produces.

### Chapter 10 — Problem 5

**(a)** The two gluconeogenic bypass reactions that account for most of the $\text{ATP}$ cost are:

*Pyruvate → PEP (the first bypass):* Pyruvate carboxylase: $\text{pyruvate} + \text{CO}_2 + \text{ATP} \rightarrow \text{oxaloacetate} + \text{ADP} + P_i$ (1 $\text{ATP}$ consumed). PEPCK: $\text{oxaloacetate} + \text{GTP} \rightarrow \text{PEP} + \text{CO}_2 + \text{GDP}$ (1 GTP consumed). Total for this bypass: 1 $\text{ATP}$ + 1 GTP = 2 high-energy phosphate equivalents per carbon pair (since the investment phase generates 2 pyruvate per glucose, the cost doubles: 2 $\text{ATP}$ + 2 GTP = 4 phosphate equivalents for both pyruvate molecules).

*Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate → fructose-6-phosphate (the second bypass):* FBPase-1 hydrolyzes $\text{F-1,6-BP} \rightarrow \text{F-6-P} + P_i$. This is a simple hydrolysis; it consumes no $\text{ATP}$ or GTP directly. However, the reverse reaction in glycolysis (PFK-1) consumed 1 $\text{ATP}$ to make F-1,6-BP; bypassing it by hydrolysis means 1 $\text{ATP}$ equivalent is lost as heat (it is not regenerated). This is 1 $\text{ATP}$ equivalent dissipated per glucose synthesized.

Total: 2 $\text{ATP}$ (pyruvate carboxylase × 2) + 2 GTP (PEPCK × 2) + 1 $\text{ATP}$ (FBPase net) + 1 $\text{ATP}$ equivalent at glucose-6-phosphatase (the glucose-6-phosphate → glucose step also bypasses hexokinase's $\text{ATP}$ by simple hydrolysis) = 4 $\text{ATP}$ + 2 GTP = 6 high-energy phosphate equivalents total.

**(b)** If glycolysis and gluconeogenesis ran simultaneously at full speed in the same cell, the net reaction would be:

$\text{Glucose}\ (\text{glycolysis}) \rightarrow 2\ \text{pyruvate} + 2\ \text{ATP} + 2\ \text{NADH}$
$2\ \text{pyruvate} \rightarrow \text{Glucose}\ (\text{gluconeogenesis}) + 6\ \text{ATP consumed} + 2\ \text{NADH consumed} + 2\ \text{GTP consumed}$

Net: Glucose → glucose, with consumption of 4 $\text{ATP}$ + 2 GTP = 6 high-energy phosphate equivalents wasted per cycle. This is a futile cycle: no net synthesis or degradation of glucose occurs, and the only chemical result is the hydrolysis of 4 $\text{ATP}$ and 2 GTP to $\text{ADP}$/GDP + $P_i$, generating heat.

**(c)** Futile cycling is prevented in the liver by reciprocal allosteric regulation of the opposing enzyme pairs. The key pair is PFK-1 (glycolysis) and FBPase-1 (gluconeogenesis). The same signals activate one and inhibit the other:

- AMP activates PFK-1 and inhibits FBPase-1 (low energy state favors glycolysis).
- $\text{ATP}$ inhibits PFK-1 and activates FBPase-1 (high energy state favors gluconeogenesis).
- Fructose-2,6-bisphosphate (F-2,6-BP) potently activates PFK-1 and inhibits FBPase-1. F-2,6-BP is made by the bifunctional enzyme PFK-2/FBPase-2, which is regulated hormonally: insulin activates the kinase domain (more F-2,6-BP → pro-glycolytic), while glucagon activates PKA, which phosphorylates the bifunctional enzyme to activate the phosphatase domain (less F-2,6-BP → pro-gluconeogenic). Citrate activates FBPase-1 and inhibits PFK-1. In this way, the intracellular energy charge, the AMP/$\text{ATP}$ ratio, and hormonal signals (glucagon vs. insulin) ensure only one pathway predominates at any given moment.

### Chapter 10 — Problem 6

**(a)** Converting pyruvate to PEP requires two reactions, both occurring in the mitochondria and cytoplasm:

**Step 1 — Pyruvate carboxylase (mitochondrial matrix):** $\text{pyruvate} + \text{CO}_2 + \text{ATP} \rightarrow \text{oxaloacetate} + \text{ADP} + P_i$. Cofactor required: biotin (covalently bound to the enzyme). This reaction costs 1 $\text{ATP}$ per pyruvate. OAA cannot cross the inner mitochondrial membrane directly, so it is converted to malate by mitochondrial malate dehydrogenase (using $\text{NADH}$), exported via the malate-aspartate shuttle component, and reconverted to OAA in the cytoplasm.

**Step 2 — PEPCK (primarily cytoplasmic, though some is mitochondrial):** $\text{oxaloacetate} + \text{GTP} \rightarrow \text{PEP} + \text{CO}_2 + \text{GDP}$. Cofactor: none beyond GTP. This reaction costs 1 GTP per OAA decarboxylated.

Total cost per pyruvate converted to PEP: 1 $\text{ATP}$ + 1 GTP. For one glucose equivalent (2 pyruvate): 2 $\text{ATP}$ + 2 GTP = 4 high-energy phosphate equivalents.

**(b)** During fasting, fatty acid oxidation generates large amounts of acetyl-CoA in the mitochondrial matrix. Pyruvate carboxylase is allosterically activated by acetyl-CoA: it is essentially inactive without it. This activation is metabolically coherent because acetyl-CoA is a signal that carbon is arriving from fat (beta-oxidation) and that the TCA cycle is receiving plenty of fuel. Under these conditions, the cell does not need to burn pyruvate for energy; instead, any pyruvate that enters the mitochondria should be diverted toward OAA and gluconeogenesis. Pyruvate carboxylase activation by acetyl-CoA ensures that pyruvate (derived from alanine transamination) is channeled into gluconeogenesis rather than into the TCA cycle via PDH. Simultaneously, PDH is inhibited by acetyl-CoA (product inhibition) and by $\text{NADH}$, reinforcing the switch: pyruvate is not oxidized, it is carboxylated. The acetyl-CoA signal thus simultaneously inhibits the catabolic route (PDH) and activates the gluconeogenic route (pyruvate carboxylase), coherently redirecting carbon flux toward glucose output.

**(c)** Muscle lacks glucose-6-phosphatase, the enzyme that converts glucose-6-phosphate to free glucose for export into the blood. Even though muscle can carry out the earlier steps of gluconeogenesis (from pyruvate to glucose-6-phosphate via the same enzymes as liver), it cannot release glucose into the circulation because glucose-6-phosphate is trapped inside the cell; it can only be used for internal glycogen synthesis or re-enter glycolysis. The liver has glucose-6-phosphatase in the ER lumen and is the only tissue that can complete gluconeogenesis and export free glucose. This single enzymatic difference is the molecular basis of the Cori cycle: muscle converts lactate and alanine (derived from glycolysis and protein catabolism) to gluconeogenic intermediates, but the final glucose export step must occur in the liver. Muscle effectively exports three-carbon precursors; the liver completes the job. The Cori cycle therefore requires functional cooperation between muscle and liver, and the absence of glucose-6-phosphatase in muscle is what makes this two-organ division of labor necessary.

### Chapter 10 — Problem 7

**(a)** In aerobic glycolysis (Warburg effect), glucose is converted to lactate despite the presence of oxygen. The net $\text{ATP}$ yield is 2 $\text{ATP}$ per glucose, the same as anaerobic glycolysis. ### Chapter 10 — Problem 8

**(a)** The student's causal claim is incorrect. Lactate itself is not an acid in the relevant sense: the LDH reaction is: $\text{pyruvate} + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^+ \rightarrow \text{lactate} + \text{NAD}^+$. One proton is consumed per lactate produced. Looking at the net reaction of anaerobic glycolysis (glucose → 2 lactate + 2 $\text{H}^+$ from $\text{ATP}$ hydrolysis), the proton generation is attributable to $\text{ATP}$ hydrolysis, not to lactate production per se. When $\text{ATP}$ is synthesized in glycolysis, it captures free energy; when that $\text{ATP}$ is subsequently hydrolyzed by cellular ATPases (to do work), it releases $\text{ADP}$ + $P_i$ + $\text{H}^+$. It is the net hydrolysis of $\text{ATP}$ under conditions where oxidative phosphorylation cannot regenerate it fast enough that generates protons and acidifies the cell. Lactate is the conjugate base of lactic acid (pKa ≈ 3.9) and is fully ionized at physiological pH; it exits the cell as lactate$^-$ with a proton cotransported by MCT transporters, and both the lactate ion and the proton contribute to what is observed clinically. However, the root cause of the acidosis is proton accumulation from $\text{ATP}$ hydrolysis, not direct acid production by LDH.

**(b)** The actual mechanism: under hypoxia, oxidative phosphorylation falls because the electron transport chain is limited by $\text{O}_2$ as the terminal electron acceptor. $\text{ATP}$ synthesis drops. The cell compensates by upregulating glycolysis (via PFK-1 activation by rising AMP). Glycolysis generates 2 $\text{ATP}$ per glucose but also produces $\text{NADH}$, which cannot be reoxidized by Complex I (ETC is impaired). To maintain glycolytic flux (which requires $\text{NAD}^+$), LDH reduces pyruvate to lactate, regenerating $\text{NAD}^+$. The net $\text{ATP}$ production per glucose is low but positive. However, as glycolysis accelerates and every $\text{ATP}$ produced is immediately hydrolyzed by energy-demanding processes (ion pumps, contractile machinery), the rate of $\text{ADP}$ + $P_i$ + $\text{H}^+$ production from $\text{ATP}$ hydrolysis exceeds the cell's buffering capacity. Protons accumulate. In severe hypoxia, cells cannot maintain ion gradients, osmotic balance fails, and cell injury occurs.

**(c)** Blood lactate is clinically useful because it is a reliable quantitative proxy for the degree to which cells are relying on anaerobic glycolysis. Even if lactate itself is not the direct acid, its rise is tightly correlated with the underlying metabolic state (high glycolytic flux, impaired oxidative metabolism, $\text{ATP}$ deficit) that produces acidosis and tissue injury. A rising lactate level tells the clinician: this patient's tissues are not receiving or using enough oxygen, the cells are in energy distress, and the systemic buffering capacity is being overwhelmed. Lactate is measurable rapidly, reproducibly, and on standard point-of-care analyzers. Physicians use it as a severity marker, to guide fluid resuscitation in sepsis and hemorrhagic shock, and as a prognostic indicator. The causal story being more complex does not reduce its clinical utility, because the mechanism is reliable: lactate rises when and because oxidative metabolism is failing, which is exactly what the clinician needs to know.

### Chapter 11 — Problem 1

**(a)** Electron carrier-producing steps per turn of the TCA cycle (starting from one acetyl-CoA condensing with one OAA):

| Step | Enzyme | Product | Reaction type |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3 | Isocitrate dehydrogenase | $\text{NADH}$ | Oxidative decarboxylation (oxidation-reduction + decarboxylation) |
| 4 | α-KG dehydrogenase complex | $\text{NADH}$ | Oxidative decarboxylation (oxidation-reduction + decarboxylation) |
| 6 | Succinate dehydrogenase (Complex II) | $\text{FADH}_2$ | Oxidation-reduction (removal of two H across a double bond) |
| 8 | Malate dehydrogenase | $\text{NADH}$ | Oxidation-reduction |

Total per turn: 3 $\text{NADH}$ + 1 $\text{FADH}_2$.

**(b)** Each $\text{NADH}$ yields approximately 2.5 $\text{ATP}$ when oxidized by the electron transport chain (Complex I → III → IV pathway, with the P/O ratio of approximately 2.5). Each $\text{FADH}_2$ yields approximately 1.5 $\text{ATP}$ (entering at Complex II, bypassing Complex I proton pumping). Per turn:

3 $\text{NADH}$ × 2.5 = 7.5 $\text{ATP}$ equivalents
1 $\text{FADH}_2$ × 1.5 = 1.5 $\text{ATP}$ equivalents
1 GTP (direct substrate-level phosphorylation at step 5) = 1 $\text{ATP}$ equivalent

Total ≈ 10 $\text{ATP}$ equivalents per turn of the TCA cycle per acetyl-CoA, and this is why the cycle generates far more than the single GTP produced directly. The GTP is a minor contributor; the oxidative power captured in $\text{NADH}$ and $\text{FADH}_2$, and subsequently harvested by the electron transport chain and oxidative phosphorylation, accounts for ~90% of the energy yield.

**(c)** The $\text{CO}_2$ released at steps 3 and 4 does not come from the acetyl-CoA that entered in the same turn. This is one of the most commonly misunderstood features of the cycle. The acetyl carbons enter at step 1 as acetyl-CoA, condensing with OAA to form citrate (C6). The carbon skeleton of citrate is asymmetric: the two carbons from acetyl-CoA end up specifically in the pro-S arm of citrate (the arm aconitase handles). However, aconitase and isocitrate dehydrogenase (step 3) act on the carbons that originated from OAA in the previous turn; the $\text{CO}_2$ released at steps 3 and 4 comes from the OAA carbons, not the newly entered acetyl carbons.

If the methyl carbon of acetyl-CoA (C2, labeled $^{14}\text{C}$) is followed for two full turns: in the first turn, both acetyl carbons are incorporated into succinate symmetrically, but the labeled carbons are not released as $\text{CO}_2$ (they pass through steps 3 and 4 intact). At the end of turn 1, the label appears in OAA at C1 and C4 (in fumarate, the symmetric intermediate distributes the label equally). In the second turn, OAA carrying the label from turn 1 enters as the acceptor; the carbons at C1 and C4 of OAA are the ones decarboxylated at steps 3 and 4. Therefore, $^{14}\text{CO}_2$ would first be released in the second turn, not the first.

**(b)** Arsenite is a trivalent arsenic compound that reacts covalently with vicinal dithiols — pairs of thiol groups positioned close together. The E2 lipoic acid prosthetic group, when in its reduced (dithiol) form, presents exactly this reactive pair. Arsenite forms a stable cyclic arsenic-sulfur ring with the two thiol groups of reduced lipoate, permanently inactivating E2. Since lipoate cycles between its dithiol and disulfide forms during every catalytic cycle, arsenite traps it in the dithiol state and blocks the cycle. Thiamine deficiency eliminates TPP, inactivating E1 and blocking the first step (decarboxylation). Both produce PDH failure and pyruvate accumulation, but the proximal block is at different subunits: thiamine deficiency blocks step 1 (decarboxylation by E1), while arsenite blocks step 3-4 (the lipoate reoxidation cycle at E2/E3). The biochemical signature differs: arsenic poisoning does not affect TPP-dependent enzymes that lack lipoate (such as transketolase), while thiamine deficiency impairs all TPP-dependent enzymes across multiple pathways.

**(c)** The E3 FAD half-reaction: FAD/$\text{FADH}_2$ (E°′ ≈ −0.29 V for the E3-bound form in PDH) donates electrons to $\text{NAD}^+$/$\text{NADH}$ (E°′ = −0.32 V).

ΔE°′ = E°′(acceptor) − E°′(donor) = −0.32 − (−0.29) = −0.03 V

$$\Delta G^{\circ'} = -(2)(96.5)(−0.03) = +5.8\;\text{kJ/mol}$$

The reaction is slightly thermodynamically unfavorable under standard conditions (ΔG°′ > 0). However, the reaction still proceeds because the reaction is driven forward by the very low [$\text{NADH}$]/[$\text{NAD}^+$] ratio in the mitochondrial matrix under active metabolism ($\text{NAD}^+$ is continuously reoxidized by Complex I), keeping Q far below the standard-state value. The E3 FAD thus serves as a redox relay with thermodynamics tuned to be close to neutral so that the reaction is reversible and sensitive to the $\text{NAD}^+$/$\text{NADH}$ ratio — a design feature that helps regulate PDH activity in response to the cell's oxidative state.

### Chapter 11 — Problem 3

**(a)** Elevated mitochondrial acetyl-CoA and $\text{NADH}$ both signal that fuel oxidation is running at high capacity: fat is being burned, the TCA cycle is processing large amounts of carbon, and the electron carriers are heavily loaded. PDH is regulated by a dedicated PDH kinase (PDK). PDK is activated by both acetyl-CoA (the product of PDH's own reaction — product inhibition) and $\text{NADH}$ (also a downstream product). When PDK is activated, it phosphorylates the E1α subunit of PDH (at one or more serine residues in the phosphorylation loop), converting the E1α subunit from the active dephosphorylated form to the inactive phosphorylated form. The result is that PDH is turned off: pyruvate no longer flows into acetyl-CoA via PDH.

**(b)** Elevated acetyl-CoA simultaneously activates pyruvate carboxylase, which converts pyruvate → OAA. These two events are complementary: PDH inhibition stops pyruvate from becoming acetyl-CoA (the TCA cycle doesn't need more fuel right now), while pyruvate carboxylase activation uses pyruvate as a gluconeogenic building block instead (OAA is a gluconeogenic intermediate). In the fasted liver, this combination makes perfect physiological sense: fat is providing all the oxidative fuel (as acetyl-CoA from beta-oxidation), so the liver does not need to burn pyruvate. The liver should instead be using pyruvate (and the alanine/lactate it receives from peripheral tissues) to make glucose for export to the brain and red blood cells. The same signal (high acetyl-CoA) simultaneously shuts the catabolic door (PDH) and opens the anabolic door (pyruvate carboxylase), redirecting the entire pyruvate pool from oxidation to gluconeogenesis.

**(c)** When gluconeogenesis is active, OAA is continuously diverted to PEP via PEPCK, depleting the matrix OAA pool. This reduces the availability of OAA for citrate synthase (step 1 of the TCA cycle), which would slow the cycle. The acetyl-CoA signal compensates: high acetyl-CoA activates pyruvate carboxylase, which generates OAA from pyruvate + $\text{CO}_2$ + $\text{ATP}$. This anaplerotic (cycle-replenishing) reaction tops up the OAA pool even while some is being drawn off. The balance between how much OAA goes to the TCA cycle versus to gluconeogenesis is determined by the relative activities of citrate synthase and PEPCK, which in turn are governed by the concentrations of their substrates and products (acetyl-CoA availability, $\text{ATP}$/$\text{ADP}$ ratio, GTP availability for PEPCK) and by the mitochondrial/cytoplasmic OAA gradient. During heavy gluconeogenesis, the demand for OAA by PEPCK is high, acetyl-CoA supports both the TCA cycle entry step (via citrate synthase) and OAA regeneration (via pyruvate carboxylase), and the system runs a partial TCA cycle sufficient to oxidize the acetyl-CoA from fat while shuttling most OAA toward glucose synthesis.

### Chapter 11 — Problem 5

**(a)** Three TCA cycle intermediates drawn off for biosynthesis (cataplerosis):

1. **Citrate:** Exported from the mitochondria into the cytoplasm via the citrate shuttle (tricarboxylate carrier). In the cytoplasm, $\text{ATP}$-citrate lyase cleaves citrate to acetyl-CoA + OAA. The cytoplasmic acetyl-CoA is the building block for fatty acid synthesis and cholesterol/isoprenoid synthesis.

2. **α-Ketoglutarate (α-KG):** Drawn off for amino acid biosynthesis, particularly as the carbon skeleton for glutamate (via glutamate dehydrogenase or aminotransferases) and from there to glutamine, proline, arginine, and other amino acids. α-KG is also used in the synthesis of hydroxyproline (collagen crosslinking) via prolyl hydroxylase.

3. **Oxaloacetate (OAA):** Used as the starting point for gluconeogenesis (OAA → PEP via PEPCK), and also transaminated to aspartate (a precursor for purine and pyrimidine nucleotide synthesis and for the urea cycle).

**(b)** Two anaplerotic routes that replenish TCA intermediates:

1. **Pyruvate carboxylase:** $\text{pyruvate} + \text{CO}_2 + \text{ATP} \rightarrow \text{OAA}$. The enzyme is biotin-dependent and activated by acetyl-CoA. This directly replenishes OAA, the acceptor for acetyl-CoA at step 1. This is the major anaplerotic route in the liver.

2. **Glutamate dehydrogenase (or transamination):** $\text{glutamate} + \text{NAD}^+ \rightarrow \alpha\text{-KG} + \text{NADH} + \text{NH}_4^+$ (glutamate dehydrogenase), or $\text{amino acid} + \text{OAA} \rightarrow \text{glutamate} + \text{keto acid}$ (aspartate aminotransferase). Both replenish α-KG. Dietary amino acids that feed into α-KG or succinyl-CoA (e.g., glutamine, histidine, arginine → α-KG; methionine, isoleucine, valine, threonine → succinyl-CoA) are also important anaplerotic substrates.

**(c)** The cell cannot perform net gluconeogenesis from acetyl-CoA because no net carbon accumulates in the cycle per turn. Each turn of the TCA cycle incorporates 2 carbons from acetyl-CoA into citrate (C6), but releases 2 carbons as $\text{CO}_2$ (one at isocitrate dehydrogenase, one at α-KG dehydrogenase). The OAA that is regenerated at the end of the cycle has the same number of carbons as the OAA that entered. There is no net gain of four-carbon intermediates: the two carbons that entered from acetyl-CoA are completely offset by the two $\text{CO}_2$ released. Since gluconeogenesis requires net three-carbon or four-carbon building blocks (pyruvate or OAA) that can be exported from the cycle, and since running the cycle with acetyl-CoA as the only carbon input produces no net increase in OAA or other precursors, no glucose can be synthesized. Mammals lack glyoxylate cycle enzymes (isocitrate lyase and malate synthase, present in plants and bacteria), which would allow the net incorporation of 2 acetyl carbons into a 4-carbon intermediate. Without those enzymes, acetyl-CoA carbons are thermodynamically committed to $\text{CO}_2$.

### Chapter 11 — Problem 6

**(a)** The primary thermodynamic mechanism is product removal by the downstream enzyme. Malate dehydrogenase (step 8) produces $\text{NADH}$ and OAA. OAA, the product, is an extremely reactive electrophile with a very low steady-state concentration in the mitochondrial matrix because it is immediately consumed by citrate synthase (step 1), which has a very large negative ΔG°′ (approximately −32 kJ/mol) for the condensation of OAA with acetyl-CoA to form citrate. Citrate synthase maintains OAA at near-nanomolar concentrations, far below the equilibrium concentration that would otherwise favor the reverse reaction (malate ← OAA). By Le Chatelier's principle, the continuous removal of OAA keeps Q for the malate dehydrogenase reaction very small, making the actual ΔG negative even though ΔG°′ is +29.7 kJ/mol. The "upstream pull" that drives malate dehydrogenase forward is the thermodynamically favorable citrate synthase step immediately downstream.

**(b)** If citrate synthase is completely inhibited, OAA can no longer be consumed. OAA would accumulate in the matrix. As [OAA] rises, the mass action ratio Q for malate dehydrogenase (Q = [$\text{NADH}$][OAA] / [malate][$\text{NAD}^+$]) increases toward and then past the equilibrium constant. Once Q exceeds Keq, the actual ΔG becomes positive and the reaction runs in reverse: OAA + $\text{NADH}$ → malate + $\text{NAD}^+$. Malate dehydrogenase would flip direction, running backward to reduce OAA back to malate. This illustrates why the cycle is dependent on the entire sequence of reactions proceeding: removing one downstream sink (citrate synthase) forces the upstream near-equilibrium step (malate dehydrogenase) into reverse.

**(c)** Malate dehydrogenase is near-equilibrium, meaning its rate and direction are exquisitely sensitive to the concentrations of its substrates and products: specifically [malate], [$\text{NAD}^+$], [$\text{NADH}$], and [OAA]. OAA in turn reflects the activity of citrate synthase (which consumes OAA) and the anaplerotic supply of OAA. When the TCA cycle is running fast (high flux through isocitrate dehydrogenase, α-KG dehydrogenase), OAA is regenerated rapidly by fumarase and malate dehydrogenase from upstream intermediates, and citrate synthase consumes it briskly. The steady-state [OAA] stays very low but proportional to how fast the cycle turns. When the cycle slows (e.g., due to $\text{NADH}$ product inhibition at steps 3 and 4), OAA regeneration slows, citrate synthase slows, and the pull on malate dehydrogenase decreases. Because malate dehydrogenase is near-equilibrium and sensitive to [OAA], the flux through it directly tracks the flux through the rest of the cycle: it acts as a thermodynamic gauge of how rapidly downstream steps are consuming OAA, and therefore as an indirect reporter of the overall cycle activity.

### Chapter 11 — Problem 7

**(a)** This is feedforward regulation: $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ signals the need for increased $\text{ATP}$ production before the demand is met. During muscle contraction, $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ is released from the sarcoplasmic reticulum to trigger actomyosin ATPase. At the same moment, the same $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ signal diffuses into the mitochondrial matrix (via the mitochondrial calcium uniporter) and activates PDH phosphatase (dephosphorylating and activating PDH), isocitrate dehydrogenase, and α-KG dehydrogenase. The result is that the TCA cycle accelerates at the same instant that the muscle begins consuming $\text{ATP}$ for contraction, anticipating the demand for $\text{NADH}$ and $\text{FADH}_2$ before $\text{NADH}$ levels fall. Feedback regulation would be the opposite pattern: the pathway would respond only after a downstream product (like AMP or low $\text{NADH}$) had already fallen below set point. Here, $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ is not a product of the pathway but the trigger for the $\text{ATP}$-consuming process; activating the $\text{ATP}$-regenerating pathway with the same signal is feedforward control.

**(b)** $\text{NADH}$ inhibition of isocitrate dehydrogenase and α-KG dehydrogenase is product inhibition, a form of negative feedback regulation. High $\text{NADH}$ means the pathway's electron carrier outputs are exceeding the capacity of the ETC to reoxidize them; the downstream sink (Complex I) is backed up. This signals that the TCA cycle is running faster than oxidative phosphorylation can handle, and slowing the cycle prevents further $\text{NADH}$ accumulation that could over-reduce the electron transport chain and inhibit ETC function.

**(c)** $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ and $\text{NADH}$ would both be high simultaneously during the transition from rest to intense exercise: $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ is high because the muscle is actively contracting, and $\text{NADH}$ may be transiently high because the ETC, although working hard, has not yet fully ramped up proton pumping and $\text{ATP}$ synthesis to match the new demand. This is a real transient tension in the regulation: the $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ signal is pushing the cycle to produce more $\text{NADH}$, but the $\text{NADH}$ signal is pushing back. The resolution is likely kinetic and hierarchical: $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ activation is rapid and dominant during the initial contraction burst, ensuring $\text{NADH}$ is produced quickly for the ETC. As exercise stabilizes and the ETC catches up (reoxidizing $\text{NADH}$ to $\text{NAD}^+$ faster), the $\text{NADH}$ inhibition decreases and both signals reach a new steady state where cycle activity is matched to ETC capacity. The cell resolves the apparent conflict not by choosing one signal over the other but by the ETC draining the $\text{NADH}$ pool, which removes the inhibitory signal and allows $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ activation to dominate at steady state. Additionally, $\text{ADP}$ rising during contraction activates Complex V and the ETC, pulling $\text{NAD}^+$ regeneration forward and relieving $\text{NADH}$ inhibition.

### Chapter 11 — Problem 8

**(a)** FAD rather than $\text{NAD}^+$ is required for succinate dehydrogenase because the thermodynamics of the succinate/fumarate couple are incompatible with $\text{NAD}^+$ reduction. Using reduction potentials:

For succinate oxidation by $\text{NAD}^+$: ΔE°′ = E°′($\text{NAD}^+$/$\text{NADH}$) − E°′(fumarate/succinate) = −0.32 − (+0.03) = −0.35 V

$$\Delta G^{\circ'} = -(2)(96.5)(-0.35) = +67.6\;\text{kJ/mol}$$

This is strongly unfavorable: electrons cannot spontaneously flow from a substrate at +0.03 V to $\text{NAD}^+$ at −0.32 V. $\text{NAD}^+$ is a weaker oxidant than needed.

For succinate oxidation by FAD: ΔE°′ = E°′(FAD/$\text{FADH}_2$) − E°′(fumarate/succinate) ≈ 0 − (+0.03) = −0.03 V, giving ΔG°′ ≈ +5.8 kJ/mol (borderline), which is pulled forward in the cell by product removal (fumarate consumption by fumarase). FAD is used because its reduction potential is close enough to the fumarate/succinate couple to make the electron transfer thermodynamically feasible under cellular conditions.

**(b)** Succinate dehydrogenase oxidizes succinate to fumarate and transfers the electrons to FAD, producing $\text{FADH}_2$ (which is enzyme-bound and immediately passes electrons to ubiquinone as part of Complex II). This bypasses Complex I and enters the electron transport chain at ubiquinone. $\text{FADH}_2$ entering at Complex II yields approximately 1.5 $\text{ATP}$ equivalents per $\text{FADH}_2$ (bypassing the proton-pumping step of Complex I). Per glucose molecule, succinate dehydrogenase runs twice (once per acetyl-CoA, and 2 acetyl-CoA per glucose). The direct $\text{ATP}$ output from this enzyme per glucose: 2 × 1.5 = 3 $\text{ATP}$ equivalents. When SDH is deficient, these 3 $\text{ATP}$ equivalents per glucose from the succinate dehydrogenase/ubiquinone step are lost. Additionally, blocking SDH also prevents the regeneration of fumarate and subsequent malate and OAA, which would slow the entire TCA cycle and reduce $\text{NADH}$ production at the other three steps, compounding the $\text{ATP}$ deficit beyond the 3 direct $\text{ATP}$ equivalents.

**(c)** When SDH is deficient, succinate accumulates inside the mitochondria and diffuses into the cytoplasm, where it acts as a competitive inhibitor of the prolyl hydroxylases (PHDs, specifically PHD1/2/3) that use α-KG as a cosubstrate. PHDs normally hydroxylate proline residues on HIF-1α, the hypoxia-inducible factor. Hydroxylation is required for VHL (an E3 ubiquitin ligase) to recognize HIF-1α and target it for proteasomal degradation. When PHDs are inhibited by succinate, HIF-1α is not hydroxylated, escapes VHL-mediated degradation, accumulates in the nucleus, and activates transcription of its target genes. These include genes for glucose transporters (GLUT1, GLUT3), glycolytic enzymes (PGK1, LDHA), erythropoietin, VEGF (angiogenesis), and other pro-survival and pro-proliferative targets. The cell phenotypically mimics hypoxia even in normoxic conditions: it increases glycolytic flux, drives angiogenesis, and resists apoptosis. Tumors arising from SDH mutations are called paragangliomas and pheochromocytomas (parasympathetic and sympathetic ganglia tumors); these are the classic SDH-associated hereditary tumor syndromes, caused by mutations in SDH subunits B, C, or D.

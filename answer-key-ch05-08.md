## Chapter 5 Answers: Carbohydrates

### Chapter 5 — Problem 1


Glucose and galactose differ only at C4, where the hydroxyl group is equatorial (glucose) versus axial (galactose) in the chair conformation. That single geometric difference is enough for enzyme discrimination because enzyme active sites are shaped to form a precise constellation of non-covalent contacts: hydrogen bonds, van der Waals interactions, and electrostatic interactions with every hydroxyl group on the sugar ring. Each hydroxyl acts as a recognition element; if it is in the wrong position, even by the small displacement between axial and equatorial, the pattern of contacts is broken.

Hexokinase's active site is complementary to the equatorial C4-OH of glucose. When galactose binds in the same orientation, its axial C4-OH clashes sterically or fails to form the expected hydrogen bond, so the substrate is not positioned correctly for phosphoryl transfer and catalytic rate drops sharply. Galactokinase has an active site shaped around the axial C4-OH geometry, so it accommodates galactose but not glucose.

The structural feature that makes the distinction possible is the precise three-dimensional geometry of the active site, which can read the configuration of individual chiral centers as distinct spatial arrangements of hydrogen-bond donors and acceptors. A single epimerization, which changes only the axial/equatorial orientation of one hydroxyl, is readable by the enzyme as a fundamentally different substrate.

### Chapter 5 — Problem 2

**(a)** The polysaccharide is essentially non-reducing. A reducing polysaccharide has a free anomeric (C1) hydroxyl at its reducing end that can open to an aldehyde and react with oxidants like $\text{Cu}^{2+}$. In a long linear or branched polymer, every internal glucose has its anomeric carbon locked in a glycosidic bond, and there is only one free reducing end per chain. For a high-molecular-weight polymer, that single reducing end constitutes an infinitesimal fraction of the total residues, so the compound is operationally non-reducing: it gives negligible or no positive result with reducing-sugar tests such as Benedict's reagent.

**(b)** Mutarotation requires a free anomeric carbon that can undergo ring-opening and equilibration between the alpha and beta forms. In the intact polysaccharide, essentially all anomeric carbons are involved in glycosidic bonds, so the ring cannot open and no optical rotation change is observed. When the chain is very large, even the single free reducing end contributes so little to the measured rotation that no change is detectable. The "isolated monosaccharide does not mutarotate" observation points to the same principle: the glucose units within the polymer chain have no accessible hemiacetal, and the polysaccharide solution behaves as though it is locked in a fixed conformation.

**(c)** This polysaccharide is cellulose. It is a linear polymer of glucose joined by β(1→4) glycosidic bonds, which is why β-glucosidase hydrolyzes it completely while α-glucosidase has no effect. Humans cannot digest cellulose because we lack the appropriate β(1→4)-glucosidase (cellulase) in our digestive tracts. Our intestinal and pancreatic carbohydrases (salivary amylase, pancreatic amylase, maltase, sucrase-isomaltase) are all specific for α-glycosidic linkages. The β-glucosidic bonds of cellulose are entirely inert to these enzymes, so cellulose passes through the small intestine undigested and enters the colon as dietary fiber.

### Chapter 5 — Problem 3

The student's objection confuses storage capacity with storage utility. For glycogen in muscle and liver, the physiologically important variable is not how much glucose can be packed into the granule but how fast glucose can be extracted from it. Those are different optimization problems with different structural solutions.

The enzyme responsible for mobilizing glycogen is glycogen phosphorylase, which releases glucose-1-phosphate by phosphorolytic cleavage of α(1→4) bonds. Crucially, phosphorylase acts only at non-reducing ends; it cannot initiate cleavage at an interior residue or at a branch point. It peels glucose units off the end of a chain one at a time until it reaches four residues from a branch, then it stalls and waits for the debranching enzyme to act.

This means the rate of glucose mobilization is proportional to the number of non-reducing ends simultaneously accessible to phosphorylase. In a linear polymer or in lightly branched amylopectin, there is one non-reducing end per chain and a small number of branch termini. In glycogen, which branches roughly every 8 to 12 residues compared to amylopectin's every 24 to 30, the number of non-reducing ends is roughly three to four times greater per unit of polymer mass. More branch points directly create more free chain termini, and each terminus is a docking site for one phosphorylase molecule. The highly branched structure is a strategy for maximizing the number of enzyme access points, not for maximizing raw storage density.

The student's concern about "wasted glucose" conflates two things. Yes, shorter outer chains mean the phosphorylase processivity per chain is shorter before it stalls. But the cell does not need to mobilize the entire granule at once; it needs a burst of glucose-1-phosphate over a few seconds in response to neural or hormonal signals. A glycogen granule with 2,000 non-reducing ends can support 2,000 simultaneously active phosphorylase molecules. An amylopectin molecule of the same mass with 500 ends supports only 500. The maximum glucose output per second scales with the number of termini, not the total stored glucose. A polymer that prioritizes long chains at the expense of branching would be slower to mobilize regardless of how much glucose it contained.

For muscle during intense exercise, ATP turnover must be sustained within 1 to 2 seconds; for liver during hypoglycemia, blood glucose must be restored within minutes. Both tissues need rate, not mass. The higher branching frequency of glycogen is therefore a functional adaptation precisely for rapid glucose availability, not evidence of metabolic inefficiency.

### Chapter 5 — Problem 4

Benedict's reagent detects reducing sugars: molecules that contain a free anomeric carbon capable of ring-opening to expose an aldehyde (or in the case of ketoses, a reactive carbonyl that can isomerize to an aldehyde). The test is positive when the anomeric carbon is not committed to a glycosidic bond and can therefore reduce the $\text{Cu}^{2+}$ reagent.

In maltose, glucose is joined by an α(1→4) glycosidic bond: the anomeric C1 of one glucose is linked to C4 of the second glucose. This uses only one C1 — the other glucose retains a free C1 (its anomeric carbon is not in a glycosidic bond). That free anomeric carbon can open to the ring-open aldehyde form, giving a positive Benedict's result. Maltose is a reducing disaccharide.

In sucrose, the glycosidic bond is α(1→2β): the anomeric C1 of glucose is linked directly to the anomeric C2 of fructose. Both anomeric carbons are committed to the bond simultaneously. Neither monomer has a free anomeric carbon; neither can ring-open to an aldehyde. Sucrose is a non-reducing disaccharide, and it gives no reaction with Benedict's reagent.

The key structural distinction is which carbons are involved in the glycosidic bond. Any disaccharide that connects two anomeric carbons directly (anomeric C to anomeric C) will be non-reducing. Any disaccharide that connects an anomeric carbon to a non-anomeric hydroxyl (as in the 1→4 bond of maltose) leaves the other anomeric carbon free, making the disaccharide a reducing sugar.

### Chapter 5 — Problem 5

**(a)** The researcher should use β(1→4) glycosidic linkages, the same bond found in cellulose. Human small intestinal enzymes (salivary amylase, pancreatic amylase, maltase, sucrase-isomaltase, lactase) are all specific for α-glycosidic bonds; they cannot hydrolyze β(1→4) glucose-glucose linkages. A linear β(1→4)-glucan will therefore pass through the entire small intestine without being absorbed or cleaved. Colonic bacteria, in contrast, encode a wide array of β-glucosidases and cellulases, allowing them to ferment β-linked polysaccharides that the host cannot touch.

**(b)** The fermentation of the β-glucan by colonic bacteria produces hydrogen gas ($\text{H}_2$) as a byproduct of anaerobic carbohydrate metabolism. The $\text{H}_2$ is absorbed across the colonic mucosa into the portal circulation and ultimately exhaled from the lungs. The diagnostic test measures exhaled breath $\text{H}_2$ concentration using a gas analyzer. This principle is essentially identical to the clinical hydrogen breath test, used to diagnose lactose intolerance, fructose malabsorption, or small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO). In each case, a substrate that the host cannot absorb reaches colonic bacteria, whose fermentation produces detectable breath $\text{H}_2$.

**(c)** Subjects who produce no detectable breath $\text{H}_2$ after ingesting the drug are likely "non-producers": individuals whose colonic flora is dominated by methane-producing archaea (methanogens) rather than hydrogen-producing bacteria. Methanogens consume $\text{H}_2$ as a substrate in the reaction $\text{CO}_2 + 4\text{H}_2 \rightarrow \text{CH}_4 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$, so any $\text{H}_2$ generated by bacterial fermentation is immediately scavenged and converted to methane before it can be absorbed and exhaled. Non-producers would need a separate breath methane measurement to confirm that colonic fermentation actually occurred. Alternatively, subjects who had recently taken broad-spectrum antibiotics might also fail to produce $\text{H}_2$ due to temporary depletion of the fermenting flora.

### Chapter 5 — Problem 6

**(a)** A carboxylate with pKa ≈ 3 is overwhelmingly ionized at pH 7.4. Using the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation, the ratio of ionized to protonated form at pH 7.4 is $10^{(7.4-3)} = 10^{4.4} \approx 25{,}000$ to 1. Essentially 100% of the glucuronate carboxylates are deprotonated and carry a negative charge at physiological pH. Because each repeating disaccharide unit also carries one or more sulfate groups (pKa < 1, fully ionized under all physiological conditions), every monomer unit of a typical GAG chain contributes two or more negative charges. A long GAG chain is therefore a dense polyanion carrying dozens to hundreds of negative charges.

**(b)** The polyanionic character of GAGs generates a Donnan effect: the fixed negative charges on the chain cannot diffuse out of the extracellular matrix, so counterions ($\text{Na}^+$, $\text{K}^+$) accumulate in the matrix to maintain electroneutrality. These dissolved cations raise the osmotic pressure of the matrix relative to the surrounding fluid, drawing water in by osmosis. The water influx, opposed by the mechanical properties of collagen fibers, creates a swollen, hydrated gel that physically occupies a large volume. Under compressive load in cartilage, water is transiently expelled, but the high fixed charge density and the osmotic driving force immediately resist the compression and restore hydration when the load is removed. GAGs thus act as a reversible hydraulic shock absorber.

**(c)** GAGs are not catalytic, but they are extremely bulky, highly hydrated polyanions. In mucopolysaccharidoses, the degradative lysosomal enzymes are absent, so GAG chains that are normally turned over daily cannot be cleaved. Intact GAG chains accumulate within lysosomes, causing progressive physical distension of the organelle. Ballooned lysosomes physically crowd other organelles, disrupt membrane integrity, block normal lysosomal recycling of other substrates (secondary storage), and can trigger lysosomal membrane permeabilization, releasing hydrolases into the cytoplasm. The pathology is mechanical and biochemical congestion: the cell's recycling machinery is clogged, not because the GAG does anything actively harmful, but because an undigested macromolecule of that size and water-binding capacity cannot be accommodated in any other cellular compartment.

### Chapter 5 — Problem 7

**(a)** Type O individuals lack functional glycosyltransferase enzymes that would modify the H antigen further. Specifically, type A individuals have an α-1,3-N-acetylgalactosaminyltransferase that adds GalNAc to the terminal galactose of the H antigen; type B individuals have an α-1,3-galactosyltransferase that adds Gal to the same acceptor. Type O individuals carry loss-of-function mutations in both transferase genes (they are homozygous for the O allele, which encodes a nonfunctional enzyme), so neither sugar is added and the H antigen remains unmodified.

**(b)** Anti-A and anti-B antibodies in type O individuals arise without any prior transfusion because the carbohydrate epitopes of the A and B antigens are also present on the surface of environmental bacteria and in foods. The immune system is constantly exposed to these foreign carbohydrate structures from infancy onward, and since self-tolerance is established only for the H antigen (what is present on the individual's own red cells), the immune system mounts a B-cell response against the A and B epitopes it does not self-express. These "naturally occurring" antibodies are predominantly IgM and are maintained by ongoing low-level environmental antigen exposure, not by transfusion history.

**(c)** To convert type A red cells to type O-like cells, one would need an α-N-acetylgalactosaminidase to cleave the terminal GalNAc from the A-antigen chain. For type B cells, an α-galactosidase would cleave the terminal Gal from the B-antigen chain. In both cases, trimming the outermost sugar would expose the underlying H antigen. The treated cells would display only the H antigen and would be antigenically equivalent to type O cells, theoretically compatible with recipients carrying anti-A and anti-B antibodies. This approach has been demonstrated in principle and is an active area of blood banking research; the challenge is ensuring complete conversion and confirming that partially converted cells do not still carry residual A or B epitopes that could trigger a reaction.

### Chapter 5 — Problem 8

**(a)** The most likely diagnosis is classic galactosemia due to deficiency of galactose-1-phosphate uridylyltransferase (GALT). When galactose enters the hepatocyte after lactose digestion, galactokinase phosphorylates it to galactose-1-phosphate (Gal-1-P). Normally, GALT transfers the galactose moiety to UDP-glucose, producing UDP-galactose and glucose-1-phosphate. Without GALT, Gal-1-P accumulates and is the principal toxic intermediate. Galactitol (produced by aldose reductase acting on galactose) also accumulates in the lens and other tissues.

**(b)** The infant was entirely healthy at birth because placental transfer of glucose and postnatal intravenous dextrose provided no galactose. The galactose metabolic pathway was never stressed. Breast milk is approximately 7% lactose, a disaccharide of glucose and galactose. Once breastfeeding began, intestinal lactase cleaved lactose to glucose and galactose, and absorbed galactose entered the hepatic Leloir pathway. Without GALT, Gal-1-P immediately began accumulating in the liver, inhibiting phosphoglucomutase and UDP-glucose consumption, ultimately causing hepatocellular injury, cholestasis (jaundice), and rising transaminases.

**(c)** Glucose-oxidase dipstick tests use glucose oxidase, an enzyme that is absolutely specific for D-glucose. It catalyzes the oxidation of glucose to gluconolactone, producing $\text{H}_2\text{O}_2$ that drives a colorimetric reaction. No other sugar is oxidized by this enzyme. Galactose, though a reducing sugar, is not a substrate for glucose oxidase and gives a negative result. Benedict's reagent uses alkaline $\text{Cu}^{2+}$, which is reduced to $\text{Cu}_2\text{O}$ by any free reducing sugar (any compound with a free anomeric carbon that can open to an aldehyde). Galactose has a free C1 and gives a strong positive result. Other reducing sugars that Benedict's detects but glucose-oxidase does not: fructose, lactose, maltose, galactose, arabinose, xylose, and any other aldose or ketose with an accessible anomeric carbon.

**(d)** Even on a strict diet eliminating lactose and galactose, some patients develop long-term neurological deficits, cognitive impairment, speech difficulties, and premature ovarian insufficiency. The biochemical explanation is endogenous galactose synthesis. Cells can produce galactose internally via the epimerization of UDP-glucose to UDP-galactose by UDP-galactose-4-epimerase, followed by hydrolysis or transfer reactions that regenerate free galactose or Gal-1-P. This endogenous production operates regardless of dietary intake. Over years, even low-level Gal-1-P accumulation in the brain and ovary causes tissue damage: the brain requires precise galactosylation of glycoproteins and glycolipids for normal myelination and neuronal function, and ongoing Gal-1-P toxicity in ovarian germ cells leads to follicular depletion. Dietary restriction eliminates the acute hepatic crisis but cannot fully suppress the steady-state endogenous flux through galactose metabolism.

## Chapter 6 Answers: Lipids and Biological Membranes

### Chapter 6 — Problem 1

Triacylglycerols store roughly 37 kJ/g, while glycogen stores roughly 4 to 5 kJ/g when accounting for its associated water. The difference arises from two independent factors.

The first factor is the degree of reduction of the carbon skeleton. Fatty acids are highly reduced: their carbons carry mostly C-H bonds, and oxidizing them to $\text{CO}_2$ releases a large amount of energy because the electrons have a large drop in potential from C-H to the electron carriers and ultimately to water. Glucose carbons are partially oxidized already, carrying C-OH and C=O groups. The chemical work already done to produce those polar groups means less free energy is available on subsequent oxidation.

The second factor is hydration. Glycogen is stored in cells in association with roughly 3 to 4 grams of water per gram of glycogen due to the extensive hydrogen bonding between the polar hydroxyl groups of glucose residues and surrounding water molecules. Triacylglycerols are entirely nonpolar: they carry no hydroxyl groups and are stored in anhydrous lipid droplets with essentially no associated water. When comparing energy per gram of stored fuel on a wet-weight basis, glycogen's effective energy density is reduced by this extra water mass, while fat's energy content is quoted per gram of pure lipid. If glycogen were stored anhydrous, the gap would narrow; it is the combination of partial oxidation and mandatory hydration that makes glycogen so much less energy-dense than fat.

### Chapter 6 — Problem 2

**(a)** A glycerophospholipid has a phosphate-containing head group esterified to the sn-3 position of glycerol, with a polar moiety (choline, ethanolamine, serine, etc.) attached to the phosphate. This polar head group is entirely absent in a triacylglycerol, which esterifies all three hydroxyl groups of glycerol with fatty acids. The phosphate head group is what confers amphipathic character: it is strongly hydrophilic and ionic, while the fatty acid chains are hydrophobic. Triacylglycerols, with no polar group, are uniformly hydrophobic.

**(b)** The difference between bilayer and micelle formation comes down to molecular geometry. Phospholipids have two acyl chains at positions sn-1 and sn-2, giving them a roughly cylindrical cross-sectional shape: the area of the head group is approximately equal to the combined area of the two tails. Cylinders pack most efficiently into flat sheets where molecules are side by side with heads facing water and tails facing inward, forming a bilayer. Free fatty acids have only one acyl chain and a relatively small carboxylate head group: the molecule is cone-shaped, wide at the head and tapering toward the tail. Cones pack naturally into a sphere with the wide heads on the outside and the tips pointing inward — a micelle. The curvature of the aggregate is determined by the molecular shape ratio, and phospholipids' symmetric cross-sections favor flat bilayer geometry.

**(c)** Lysophosphatidylcholine has had one fatty acid chain hydrolytically removed, leaving a single acyl chain at sn-1 or sn-2 with the large phosphocholine head group intact. With only one tail, its geometry is now cone-shaped, analogous to a free fatty acid (though with a bulkier head). Prediction: it would form micelles, not bilayers. The single chain cannot fill the hydrophobic core of a flat bilayer leaflet without leaving a gap on one side; the spontaneous curvature drives assembly into spherical micelles. This is also why lysolipids are membrane-lytic: they insert into bilayers and introduce curvature stress that destabilizes the planar structure.

### Chapter 6 — Problem 3

**(a)** Cells adapted to 20°C must maintain membrane fluidity at a lower temperature: this is homeoviscous adaptation. Two expected differences between preparation B (20°C-adapted) and preparation A (37°C-adapted):

First, preparation B will have a higher proportion of unsaturated fatty acids. Cis double bonds introduce kinks in the acyl chains, preventing them from packing closely together, and lowering the phase transition temperature. At 20°C, without this adjustment, a membrane built primarily with saturated chains would solidify or become too viscous to support normal protein function. Cells grown at lower temperature upregulate fatty acid desaturase activity, introducing more double bonds.

Second, preparation B will likely have shorter average acyl chain lengths. Shorter chains have less surface area for Van der Waals contact between neighboring molecules, reducing cohesion and lowering the melting temperature. Both changes accomplish the same goal: preserve a liquid-disordered bilayer state at a temperature that would otherwise favor gel-phase (solid) behavior.

**(b)** Cholesterol has a dual and context-dependent effect on membrane fluidity. In a fluid membrane above the phase transition temperature, cholesterol intercalates between the acyl chains and partially orders them, reducing fluidity from the highly disordered state. In a gel-phase membrane below the transition, cholesterol disrupts tight chain packing and increases fluidity. Preparation B at 20°C is already fluid (its high unsaturation content keeps it above any gel transition at that temperature). Adding cholesterol to this already-fluid, highly unsaturated membrane would shift it toward lower fluidity at 20°C: cholesterol would order and condense the acyl chains, partially opposing the disorder conferred by the unsaturated lipids.

**(c)** The lipid-soluble drug diffuses through the hydrophobic core of the bilayer; the rate depends on how readily the acyl chains part to allow solute passage. Preparation B, with its higher unsaturation and lower overall chain packing, is more fluid than preparation A when both are examined at the same temperature. Greater fluidity means more rapid lateral and transverse movement of lipids and greater permeability to small nonpolar solutes. The drug would diffuse faster through preparation B.

### Chapter 6 — Problem 4

**(a)** Flippase (a P4-type ATPase) actively translocates phosphatidylserine from the outer (exoplasmic) leaflet to the inner (cytoplasmic) leaflet. It requires ATP. Moving PS across the bilayer is energetically costly for two reasons: the polar, charged head group of PS must traverse the hydrophobic core of the membrane (an energetically unfavorable journey), and flippase works against any gradient that would tend to distribute PS toward the outer leaflet. ATP hydrolysis drives the conformational changes that carry the lipid head group through the transporter's hydrophilic channel across the membrane.

**(b)** When scramblase is activated during apoptosis, it dissipates lipid asymmetry in an energy-independent, bidirectional fashion, allowing PS to accumulate on the outer leaflet. Externalized PS is a universal "eat-me" signal. Macrophages express cell-surface PS receptors (including TIM-4 and others) that bind the exposed PS and trigger phagocytic engulfment of the apoptotic cell before it can undergo secondary necrosis. This efferocytosis is immunologically anti-inflammatory: the apoptotic cell is removed cleanly without releasing intracellular danger signals into the surrounding tissue. This is how billions of cells are silently eliminated during normal tissue remodeling, embryonic development, and immune cell selection without triggering inflammation.

**(c)** Platelet activation triggers scramblase (TMEM16F)-mediated PS externalization on the outer leaflet. This anionic phospholipid surface serves as the catalytic platform for the prothrombinase complex (Factor Xa, Factor Va, $\text{Ca}^{2+}$, prothrombin). The negatively charged PS concentrates the positively charged clotting factors by electrostatic attraction and provides the correct geometric orientation for prothrombin cleavage, accelerating thrombin generation by roughly 1,000-fold compared to solution-phase kinetics. Without scramblase, PS remains on the inner leaflet, the prothrombinase complex cannot assemble on the platelet surface, thrombin generation is severely impaired, and clot formation fails. The patient bleeds despite a normal platelet count because the functional defect is in the procoagulant surface, not in platelet number. This condition is known as Scott syndrome.

### Chapter 6 — Problem 5

**(a)** This disorder follows autosomal recessive inheritance. Both parents are phenotypically unaffected heterozygous carriers with 50% enzyme activity, which is sufficient for normal cellular function (enzyme activity in heterozygotes exceeds the threshold required to degrade sphingomyelin as fast as it is produced). The child received one loss-of-function allele from each carrier parent and has no functional acid sphingomyelinase. For an autosomal recessive condition, two phenotypically normal parents who are both carriers have a 1-in-4 chance of producing an affected child with each pregnancy.

**(b)** Sphingomyelin consists of a ceramide backbone (sphingosine linked to a fatty acid via an amide bond at the 2-amino group) with a phosphocholine head group attached to the 1-hydroxyl of sphingosine via a phosphodiester bond. Acid sphingomyelinase hydrolyzes the phosphoester bond connecting the 1-OH of ceramide to the phosphocholine group, releasing ceramide and phosphocholine. The bond cleaved is the phosphodiester linkage on the ceramide side (between C1-O and phosphorus), analogous in type to the bond cleaved by phospholipase C on glycerophospholipids.

Sphingomyelin-rich membrane domains are continuously internalized via endocytosis, delivered to late endosomes, and then to lysosomes, where acid sphingomyelinase — optimally active at pH ~4.5 — cleaves it. When this enzyme is absent, sphingomyelin arriving via the endocytic pathway cannot be processed and accumulates in lysosomes. Glycerophospholipids with ester-linked chains and more heterogeneous saturation produce a more fluid, less ordered membrane. Sphingomyelin's preference for ordered phase behavior and its capacity for dense packing make it a better insulating lipid for the specialized requirements of the myelinated nervous system.

### Chapter 6 — Problem 6

**(a)** Three peaks above the transmembrane threshold indicate three transmembrane (TM) segments. A protein with three TM segments crosses the bilayer three times, generating four non-TM segments: an N-terminal region, two inter-TM loops, and a C-terminal region. Because the number of TM crossings is odd, the N-terminus and C-terminus reside on opposite faces of the membrane. The four non-TM segments divide two on each side: 2 intracellular and 2 extracellular (including the two termini on opposite sides, and the two loops).

By the topology established above, loop 2-3 is the extracellular loop; it would have faced the ER lumen during synthesis.

### Chapter 6 — Problem 7

**(a)** Applying $\Delta P = 2\gamma/r$: alveolus X (r = 100 µm) has $\Delta P_X = 2\gamma/100 = 0.02\gamma$, and alveolus Y (r = 50 µm) has $\Delta P_Y = 2\gamma/50 = 0.04\gamma$. Alveolus Y has the higher internal pressure. When two connected compartments have different pressures, gas flows from higher pressure to lower: air will move from Y (smaller, higher $\Delta P$) into X (larger, lower $\Delta P$). This is a positive feedback loop. As Y loses volume, its radius decreases further, its $\Delta P$ rises further, flow out accelerates, and the alveolus collapses completely. X over-inflates. Without surfactant, all small alveoli would be thermodynamically unstable and would empty into larger ones.

**(b)** Pulmonary surfactant specifically prevents this collapse because its surface tension is not constant: $\gamma$ decreases dramatically when the monolayer is compressed, i.e., when the alveolus shrinks during exhalation. As alveolus Y contracts, surfactant molecules become more densely packed on its surface, driving $\gamma$ down. If $\gamma$ falls proportionally to r, $\Delta P = 2\gamma/r$ stays approximately constant regardless of alveolar size. A smaller alveolus no longer has higher internal pressure than a larger one; the pressure differential that would drive collapse disappears. Surfactant therefore stabilizes all alveoli at comparable pressures across the range of breathing volumes.

**(c)** DPPC works as a surfactant because of its saturated palmitoyl chains. At body temperature, DPPC is near its gel-to-fluid transition: under compression during exhalation, DPPC molecules pack into a rigid, nearly crystalline monolayer that achieves surface tensions approaching zero mN/m. This extreme surface tension reduction is the key surfactant property. A phosphatidylcholine with two unsaturated chains would have a much lower phase transition temperature and would remain in a disordered liquid-crystalline state even when highly compressed; disordered chains cannot pack tightly enough to achieve comparably low surface tensions. DPPC's saturated chains are not a flaw in terms of membrane fluidity, because surfactant is not embedded in a bilayer where fluidity matters, it is a monolayer at the air-liquid interface where the ability to form a tightly packed film under pressure is the functional requirement.

### Chapter 7 — Problem 7

**(a)** Scenario A (anti-nAChR antibodies, 70% receptor loss): decremental response. With fewer functional receptors at the endplate, each release of ACh produces a smaller endplate potential. Under repetitive stimulation, the presynaptic terminal progressively depletes its readily releasable pool of ACh vesicles faster than it can replenish them, so each successive stimulus releases less ACh and the already-reduced receptor number compounds the deficit. Each successive compound muscle action potential (CMAP) is smaller.

Scenario B (synaptobrevin cleaved by toxin): decremental response, though by a different mechanism. Synaptobrevin is the v-SNARE required for vesicle fusion with the presynaptic membrane. Without it, vesicles cannot dock and fuse; ACh release fails entirely or is severely reduced from the start. Repetitive stimulation cannot overcome this because the fusion machinery is irreversibly destroyed. Each successive stimulus fails to release ACh, and successive CMAPs decrease or disappear.

(Note: an incremental response is characteristic of Lambert-Eaton syndrome, where anti-VGCC antibodies reduce initial ACh release but repetitive stimulation mobilizes calcium and transiently improves release. Neither scenario here presents that mechanism.)

**(b)** Pyridostigmine inhibits acetylcholinesterase, slowing the degradation of ACh in the synaptic cleft. Pyridostigmine would not benefit Scenario B patients.

**(c)** In Scenario A (myasthenia gravis), the residual functional nAChRs at the endplate are few. Rest allows the presynaptic terminal to replenish its ACh stores and restore the vesicle pool, rebuilding the safety margin temporarily.

### Chapter 7 — Problem 8

**(a)** In unmyelinated axons, voltage-gated $\text{Na}^+$ channels are distributed along the entire axon membrane; each segment of membrane must regenerate the action potential through local depolarization and ion flow, and conduction is slow (continuous conduction). In myelinated axons, the myelin sheath electrically insulates the internodal membrane and concentrates $\text{Na}^+$ channels at the nodes of Ranvier. The action potential jumps from node to node (saltatory conduction), regenerating only at nodes rather than at every point along the fiber. This is far faster because a large depolarizing current passively spreads through the low-resistance internodal segment almost instantaneously before regenerating at the next node. When myelin is lost, the internodal membrane is exposed but lacks adequate $\text{Na}^+$ channels; current leaks out across the now-uninsulated membrane and decays before reaching the next node. Conduction slows, becomes unreliable, or fails (conduction block).

**(b)** Weeks to months after demyelination, $\text{Na}^+$ channels can redistribute along the previously bare internodal axon membrane, partially restoring the ability to depolarize the exposed segments. This redistribution likely involves upregulation of $\text{Na}^+$ channel gene expression (particularly Nav1.2 and Nav1.6) and integration of new channels into the internodal membrane. With channels now present between nodes, the axon can conduct by a slow, continuous mechanism analogous to unmyelinated fibers, partially restoring signal transmission. This is one molecular basis for the partial spontaneous recovery observed in relapsing-remitting MS.

**(c)** Elevated temperature accelerates the inactivation kinetics of voltage-gated $\text{Na}^+$ channels: channels open more briefly and recover from inactivation more slowly at higher temperatures. In a normal myelinated axon, conduction at each node is robust because the node concentrates a high density of $\text{Na}^+$ channels; even with somewhat shortened channel open times, there is a large safety margin and conduction succeeds. In a demyelinated axon conducting through newly redistributed internodal channels at low density, the safety margin is already minimal. At elevated temperature, the shortened $\text{Na}^+$ channel open times reduce the inward current per action potential; this small current, trying to propagate through a low-density, low-efficiency internodal membrane, now fails to exceed threshold at the next regeneration point. Conduction block occurs at temperatures that would be completely tolerable in a normal myelinated axon.

## Chapter 8 Answers: Signal Transduction

### Chapter 8 — Problem 1

**(a)** Epinephrine binds the β₂-adrenergic receptor (β₂AR), a GPCR, inducing a conformational change in the receptor that activates the associated heterotrimeric G protein Gₛ. Activated receptor acts as a GEF (guanine nucleotide exchange factor): it catalyzes release of GDP from Gαs and binding of GTP. GTP-bound Gαs dissociates from Gβγ and binds adenylyl cyclase (a transmembrane enzyme) at the inner face of the plasma membrane. Adenylyl cyclase is activated and converts ATP to cyclic AMP (cAMP). cAMP diffuses in the cytoplasm and binds the regulatory subunits of protein kinase A (PKA), releasing the catalytic subunits, which then phosphorylate downstream targets including myosin light chain kinase (in smooth muscle, phosphorylation leads to relaxation) and other effectors that produce bronchodilation.

**(b)** Three termination mechanisms at three levels:

1. Intrinsic GTPase of Gαs: Gαs hydrolyzes GTP to GDP, converting itself back to the inactive state and dissociating from adenylyl cyclase. This is the primary timer of the G protein signal.

2. Phosphodiesterase (PDE): cytoplasmic PDEs (especially PDE4) hydrolyze cAMP to 5′-AMP, rapidly lowering cAMP concentration and inactivating PKA.

3. GRK-mediated receptor phosphorylation and β-arrestin recruitment: G protein-coupled receptor kinases (GRKs) phosphorylate the activated β₂AR on intracellular serine/threonine residues. β-Arrestin binds the phosphorylated receptor, sterically blocking further Gₛ coupling (uncoupling) and targeting the receptor for endocytosis (internalization/downregulation).

**(c)** GTP-γ-S cannot be hydrolyzed by the intrinsic GTPase of Gαs. The Gαs subunit binds GTP-γ-S, dissociates from Gβγ, and activates adenylyl cyclase, but the GTPase hydrolysis step that would normally terminate the signal is blocked. cAMP levels would rise continuously and remain elevated. The termination mechanism bypassed is the intrinsic GTPase activity of Gαs (mechanism 1 above). The receptor phosphorylation and PDE pathways remain intact but cannot compensate for a Gαs locked permanently in the active state.

### Chapter 8 — Problem 2

**(a)** Cholera toxin ADP-ribosylates Gαs at Arg201, a residue in the active site that is critical for GTP hydrolysis. The ADP-ribose group added to this arginine sterically and electrostatically disrupts the catalytic mechanism for water-mediated GTP hydrolysis, permanently abolishing GTPase activity. Gαs remains GTP-bound, continuously active, and continuously stimulates adenylyl cyclase, leading to pathological cAMP overproduction (particularly in intestinal epithelial cells, driving secretory diarrhea).

**(b)** Pertussis toxin ADP-ribosylates Gαi at a cysteine near the C-terminus, preventing Gαi from interacting with activated GPCRs. This locks Gαi in the GDP-bound inactive state: it cannot exchange GDP for GTP, cannot dissociate from Gβγ, and cannot inhibit adenylyl cyclase. Normally, Gαi (activated by Gi-coupled receptors) inhibits adenylyl cyclase and lowers cAMP. When Gαi is locked off, its inhibitory brake on adenylyl cyclase is removed. Adenylyl cyclase runs uninhibited, and cAMP rises. The outcome — elevated cAMP — is the same as locking Gαs on, but the mechanism is removing inhibition rather than adding stimulation.

**(c)** In a Gαi-knockout cell line, treating with a Gi-coupled receptor agonist produces no response: there is no Gαi to activate, so the agonist has nothing to work through. cAMP remains at baseline (no inhibition to add or remove). Adding cholera toxin to these same cells locks Gαs on, and cAMP rises dramatically from baseline. The magnitude of the cholera toxin effect would be the same as or larger than in wild-type cells expressing Gαi, because wild-type cells have some constitutive Gαi tone that partially inhibits adenylyl cyclase; the Gαi-knockout cell starts from a higher basal adenylyl cyclase activity (no tonic inhibition). With cholera toxin added on top of that already-uninhibited adenylyl cyclase, cAMP rises to a high absolute level. The effect of cholera toxin is therefore at least as large, and the absolute cAMP ceiling may be higher, in the Gαi-knockout cells.

### Chapter 8 — Problem 3

**(a)** The insulin receptor is a disulfide-linked αβ₂ heterodimer; each half contains one extracellular α subunit (insulin binding) and one transmembrane β subunit with an intracellular kinase domain. Insulin binding induces a conformational change that brings the two kinase domains into proximity. Trans-autophosphorylation means each kinase domain phosphorylates specific tyrosine residues on the other kinase domain. The first and most critical target is the activation loop tyrosine (Tyr1158/1162/1163 in humans). In the unphosphorylated state, the activation loop folds into the active site and blocks substrate access. Phosphorylation of the activation loop tyrosine stabilizes an open conformation of the activation loop, allowing substrates to bind and dramatically increasing catalytic activity. Additional phosphorylation of other intracellular tyrosines creates docking sites for adapter proteins.

**(b)** PI3K converts PIP2 (phosphatidylinositol-4,5-bisphosphate) to PIP3 (phosphatidylinositol-3,4,5-trisphosphate) by adding a phosphate to the 3-OH of the inositol ring. This membrane tethering is the basis for its function as a spatial second messenger that recruits specific proteins to the membrane. Akt recognizes PIP3 through its pleckstrin homology (PH) domain, which selectively binds the unique phosphate configuration of the 3-position phosphate on PIP3. This PH domain-PIP3 interaction recruits Akt to the inner face of the plasma membrane, where it is phosphorylated and activated by PDK1 and mTORC2.

**(c)** PTEN dephosphorylates PIP3 at the 3-position, regenerating PIP2, and thereby terminates the PIP3 signal. Without PTEN, PIP3 is not degraded and accumulates constitutively. Loss of PTEN therefore produces the same downstream outcome as a constitutively active PI3K mutation: sustained PIP3 elevation → sustained Akt activation → constitutive pro-survival and pro-growth signaling. Whether the gain is at PI3K (more PIP3 produced) or at PTEN (less PIP3 degraded), the steady-state PIP3 level rises and Akt is constitutively active.

### Chapter 8 — Problem 6

**(a)** Epinephrine is water-soluble and cannot cross the plasma membrane; its receptor (β₂AR) is on the cell surface. ### Chapter 8 — Problem 7

**(a)** β-Arrestin reduces β₂AR signaling through two mechanisms. ## Chapter 7 Answers: Membrane Channels and Pumps

### Chapter 7 — Problem 1

**(a)** The potassium channel's selectivity filter is lined with backbone carbonyl oxygens positioned to mimic the water molecules that surround $\text{K}^+$ in aqueous solution. $\text{K}^+$ in water is coordinated by 8 water molecules; as it enters the selectivity filter, the carbonyl oxygens substitute for water at precisely the correct distance for $\text{K}^+$. The coordination geometry and the spacing between carbonyl oxygens is optimal for $\text{K}^+$ (ionic radius ≈ 1.33 Å). $\text{Na}^+$ is smaller (ionic radius ≈ 0.95 Å) and is normally coordinated by water at a shorter distance. The carbonyl oxygens of the selectivity filter are too far apart to optimally coordinate $\text{Na}^+$: the filter cannot compress to the correct distance because the protein backbone is rigid. As a result, $\text{Na}^+$ in the filter is not adequately substituted for its hydration shell, which is thermodynamically very costly. The energy penalty for stripping $\text{Na}^+$'s waters of hydration without providing equivalent coordination in the filter is large (partially uncompensated dehydration energy). $\text{K}^+$ pays a smaller net energy cost because the filter provides nearly equivalent compensation. The selectivity arises not from a steric size exclusion but from an energetic penalty specific to the wrong-sized ion.

**(b)** With two $\text{K}^+$ ions in the filter simultaneously at adjacent sites, electrostatic repulsion between them provides the driving force for rapid exit. A single ion in the filter would be tightly bound (the carbonyl oxygens on both sides coordinate it stably), essentially trapped. The presence of a second ion behind it creates a like-charge repulsion that pushes the front ion toward the extracellular side and exits it from the filter. The "knock-on" mechanism — ion repulsion driving the next ion out — turns the selectivity filter from a slow, high-affinity trap into a high-throughput channel by making binding cooperative: the second ion's entry destabilizes the first ion's binding.

**(c)** The conserved threonine in the TVGYG sequence provides a side-chain hydroxyl that helps coordinate $\text{K}^+$ at the entry of the selectivity filter and contributes to the precise geometry of ion binding sites. Mutating threonine to alanine removes this hydroxyl, altering the coordination geometry at that site. The filter would no longer position the carbonyl oxygens as precisely, and the energy discrimination between $\text{K}^+$ and $\text{Na}^+$ would diminish. The channel would lose some selectivity for $\text{K}^+$ and would likely become more permeable to $\text{Na}^+$ relative to wild-type. The ion permeability ratio ($\text{K}^+$/$\text{Na}^+$) would decrease, potentially compromising the ability of the channel to set or maintain the potassium equilibrium potential.

### Chapter 7 — Problem 2

**(a)** Using $E_{\text{ion}} \approx 61.5\;\text{mV} \times \log_{10}([\text{ion}]_{\text{out}}/[\text{ion}]_{\text{in}})$:

$$E_{\text{Na}^+} = 61.5 \times \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{145}{12}\right) = 61.5 \times \log_{10}(12.08) = 61.5 \times 1.082 \approx +66.5\;\text{mV}$$

For $\text{Na}^+$: $V_m - E_{\text{Na}} = -70 - (+66.5) = -136.5\;\text{mV}$. The membrane is 136.5 mV more negative than the $\text{Na}^+$ equilibrium. $\text{Na}^+$ is driven strongly inward (both the concentration gradient and the negative interior attract $\text{Na}^+$ into the cell).

### Chapter 7 — Problem 3

**(a)** Voltage-gated $\text{Na}^+$ channels cycle through three states: closed (resting) → open → inactivated → closed (recovered). The refractory period corresponds to the interval during which channels are inactivated and cannot reopen. If the drug slows recovery from inactivation 10-fold, channels remain in the inactivated state roughly 10 times longer after each action potential. The absolute refractory period is dramatically prolonged, meaning the minimum inter-spike interval is greatly increased. Action potential firing frequency would fall substantially: the neuron simply cannot fire again until enough channels have recovered, and with 10-fold slower recovery, the maximum sustainable firing rate would drop proportionally.

**(b)** This phenomenon is use-dependent block (also called frequency-dependent or state-dependent block). Lidocaine enters cells through open $\text{Na}^+$ channels during an action potential and binds the inactivated state from the cytoplasmic side. After extracellular drug is washed out, intracellular drug that entered during channel opening remains trapped inside the cell and continues to bind inactivated channels. Neurons that were firing rapidly spent more time in the open and inactivated states, allowing more drug molecules to enter and accumulate intracellularly before washout. Quiescent neurons, with channels mostly in the closed resting state during washout, accumulated far less intracellular drug. The difference in recovery rate after washout reflects this differential intracellular drug loading based on prior firing history.

**(c)** Phenytoin is a use-dependent $\text{Na}^+$ channel blocker that binds the inactivated state with higher affinity than the resting closed state. Epileptic neurons firing at abnormally high frequencies spend an disproportionate fraction of time with channels in the open and inactivated states — the very states that phenytoin favors. Drug accumulates selectively in and around rapidly cycling channels. Normal neurons firing at physiological frequencies have their channels in the resting closed state for most of each inter-spike interval; phenytoin has low affinity for this state and does not accumulate there to the same extent. The result is a frequency-selective suppression: pathologically hyperactive neurons are silenced because their high-rate cycling loads them with drug, while normal neurons at low baseline firing rates are largely spared. This selectivity is the therapeutic basis for using phenytoin in epilepsy.

### Chapter 7 — Problem 4

**(a)** SGLT1 is a secondary active transporter and a symporter: it moves $\text{Na}^+$ and glucose in the same direction (into the cell), deriving energy from the $\text{Na}^+$ electrochemical gradient. GLUT2 is a passive transporter and a uniporter: it facilitates glucose diffusion down its concentration gradient without coupling to any ion and without consuming ATP. GLUT4 is likewise a passive uniporter for glucose once it is in the plasma membrane; the active step in GLUT4 regulation is the insulin-triggered vesicular translocation that inserts GLUT4 into the membrane, but the transport event itself is facilitated diffusion.

**(b)** The energy source for SGLT1 is the inward electrochemical gradient for $\text{Na}^+$: the low intracellular $\text{Na}^+$ concentration (12 mM) combined with the negative interior voltage creates a large driving force for $\text{Na}^+$ entry. SGLT1 couples this spontaneous $\text{Na}^+$ entry to obligate co-transport of glucose, moving glucose uphill against its own gradient. This cycle has a maximum turnover rate (Vmax) that is reached when every transporter molecule is occupied. At saturating glucose concentrations, the rate is limited by the conformational change rate, not by substrate concentration. Ion channels, in contrast, are open pores: once open, ions move through by diffusion without binding to a saturable site. Current through an open channel scales with the electrochemical driving force and the channel conductance without plateauing at a Vmax. The mechanistic difference is carrier (binding and conformational change, saturable) versus channel (open pore, non-saturable).

### Chapter 7 — Problem 5

**(a)** KCNQ1 encodes the pore-forming alpha subunit of a voltage-gated $\text{K}^+$ channel. Loss of IKs removes this repolarization reserve: the plateau is prolonged because the outward current needed to terminate it is reduced. A longer plateau = longer action potential duration = longer QT interval on the ECG. The prolonged repolarization creates an electrical window during which abnormal depolarizations (early afterdepolarizations) can trigger reentrant arrhythmias such as torsades de pointes.

**(c)** Under sympathetic stimulation (exercise, emotional stress, swimming with its cold-water autonomic reflex), catecholamines bind β₁-adrenergic receptors → cAMP rises → PKA phosphorylates KCNQ1 → IKs current is normally augmented substantially. This increase in IKs during sympathetic activation is physiologically essential: at higher heart rates, each diastolic interval is shorter, so each action potential must repolarize faster. IKs upregulation by sympathetic stimulation is the primary mechanism that shortens APD during tachycardia. When KCNQ1 is nonfunctional, sympathetic activation cannot augment IKs. As heart rate increases, the APD fails to shorten, the QT interval paradoxically remains long or increases relative to the RR interval (rate-corrected QT elongates), and arrhythmia risk rises.

**(d)** Beta-blockers block β₁-adrenergic receptors, reducing sympathetic drive to the heart. This limits heart rate elevation during stress or exercise, preventing the tachycardia that most stresses the deficient repolarization reserve. At a lower heart rate, each cycle allows more time for repolarization, and the absolute QT prolongation is less dangerous relative to the longer RR interval. Beta-blockers also prevent the catecholamine surge that, in patients with KCNQ1 mutations, unmasks arrhythmia vulnerability. The channel itself remains deficient, but by dampening the physiological trigger that most powerfully reveals that deficiency, the drug substantially reduces arrhythmic events.

**(a)** For each ion, the driving force equals the actual membrane potential minus the equilibrium potential, and the sign indicates direction of net flow (negative driving force = inward, positive = outward).

$\text{Na}^+$: $V_m - E_{\text{Na}} = -75 - (+66) = -141\;\text{mV}$. $\text{Na}^+$ is driven strongly inward: both the concentration gradient (low inside) and the electrical gradient (negative interior) pull $\text{Na}^+$ into the cell.

$\text{K}^+$: $V_m - E_{\text{K}} = -75 - (-90) = +15\;\text{mV}$. $\text{K}^+$ is driven outward: the concentration gradient pushing $\text{K}^+$ out (-75 is more positive than -90) slightly exceeds the electrical pull toward the negative interior.

**(b)** Even though $\text{K}^+$ is leaving at the same time $\text{Na}^+$ is entering, the net effect is depolarization because the driving forces are very different. $\text{Na}^+$ experiences a 141-mV driving force inward; $\text{K}^+$ experiences only a 15-mV driving force outward. Since the nAChR is nonselective (approximately equal permeability to both ions), the larger driving force on $\text{Na}^+$ produces a much larger inward $\text{Na}^+$ current than the outward $\text{K}^+$ current. The net flow of positive charge is inward, depolarizing the endplate. The channel's reversal potential (the potential at which $\text{Na}^+$ entry exactly balances $\text{K}^+$ exit) is approximately −15 mV, far depolarized from the resting −75 mV; opening nAChR moves the endplate potential toward this value.

**(c)** Rapid ACh termination is necessary because each nerve impulse should produce one brief endplate potential followed by repolarization and muscle relaxation. If ACh persists, nAChRs remain open continuously, the endplate stays depolarized, and the muscle cannot relax between impulses. Coordinated movement requires precise temporal control: a sustained depolarization is functionally paralyzing in a different way — it initially causes tetanic contraction, then depolarization block of the nAChR (receptors inactivate when persistently depolarized), leading to flaccid paralysis.

When AChE is inhibited by organophosphate compounds, ACh accumulates in the synapse. At the neuromuscular junction this causes initial fasciculations followed by flaccid weakness and eventually respiratory failure as the diaphragm becomes paralyzed by depolarization block. At muscarinic synapses (ANS, CNS), excessive ACh produces the cholinergic toxidrome: bradycardia, hypersecretions, bronchospasm, and miosis. The extreme rapidity of normal ACh clearance (under 1 ms) is therefore not merely efficient, it is the structural basis of neuromuscular precision.

## The Big Picture

Every reaction you will study in this course happens inside a cell. That statement is so obvious it barely registers, but stop on it for a moment. Biochemical reactions do not happen in an infinite solution. They happen in compartments, where concentrations are controlled, where the products of one reaction are handed directly to the next enzyme in a pathway, where proton gradients can be maintained across a barrier, where signals from outside can be transduced to responses inside. The compartment is not incidental to the chemistry. The compartment makes the chemistry possible.

The compartment exists because of lipids.

Specifically, it exists because of a class of lipid molecule called a phospholipid, which has a property shared by a handful of other biologically important lipid types: it is amphipathic. One end is hydrophilic (water-loving), the other end is hydrophobic (water-fearing). In water, amphipathic molecules do not dissolve and do not precipitate. They self-assemble. Spontaneously, without any enzyme or template, a collection of phospholipid molecules in water will form a bilayer: two sheets of molecules arranged tail-to-tail, with all the hydrophilic heads facing the water on either side and all the hydrophobic tails sequestered in the interior. This bilayer is the cell membrane, and the driving force behind its formation is simply the hydrophobic effect: the energetically unfavorable contact between hydrophobic tails and water is minimized when those tails are hidden from water inside the bilayer.

This chapter covers what lipids are chemically, how the bilayer is built and regulated, and what happens when the system fails. The two clinical anchors are surfactant deficiency in premature infants (a pure failure of a specific lipid to perform its physical function) and familial hypercholesterolemia (a failure of membrane-based cholesterol sensing that causes arterial disease in childhood). Together they illustrate the two central roles of membrane lipids: maintaining the physical properties of biological surfaces, and participating in cellular signaling and regulation.

Lipid metabolism, including the synthesis and catabolism of fatty acids, is deferred to Chapter 14. This chapter is about structure and function, not about how the molecules are made or broken down.

## Fatty Acids: The Structural Unit

### What a Fatty Acid Is

A **fatty acid** is a long hydrocarbon chain with a carboxylate group ($-COO^-$) at one end. The carboxylate end is hydrophilic; the hydrocarbon chain is hydrophobic. Fatty acids are therefore amphipathic, though they are typically not the molecules that form bilayers on their own. They are the structural subunits of the more complex lipids that do.

The hydrocarbon chain is typically 14 to 24 carbons long, with 16-carbon and 18-carbon chains predominating in mammalian biology. The chain may be fully saturated (all single bonds between carbons, maximum hydrogen content) or it may contain one or more double bonds. Fatty acids with no double bonds are **saturated fatty acids**; those with one double bond are **monounsaturated**; those with two or more double bonds are **polyunsaturated**.

Similar to carbohydrates, lipids serve a variety of roles in the cell. Famously they serve a structural role, providing the foundational layer of the plasma membrane and organelle membranes. However lipids can also serve as fuels, again similar to carbohydrates. Importantly, not only can they serve as fuel but fatty acids are the **main storage form** of energy in the body. Lastly, lipid derivatives act as components of cell signaling pathways, often acting as what are known as **second messengers**, as we will see in the cell signaling chapter.

### Naming Conventions

Fatty acid names follow a shorthand: chain length, colon, number of double bonds. **Palmitic acid** is 16:0 (16 carbons, 0 double bonds). **Stearic acid** is 18:0. **Oleic acid** is 18:1 (18 carbons, 1 double bond). Linoleic acid is 18:2. Arachidonic acid is 20:4.

When double bonds are present, their position is specified by a delta ($\Delta$) notation: $\Delta^9$ means the double bond begins at carbon 9, counting from the carboxylate carbon (C1). Oleic acid is 18:1($\Delta^9$). An alternative notation counts from the methyl end of the chain (the $\omega$ or $n$ end): omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids have their first double bond 3 or 6 carbons from the methyl end respectively. These are the "essential fatty acids" that humans cannot synthesize and must obtain from diet.

### Geometry of Double Bonds: Cis vs. Trans

Double bonds in biological fatty acids are almost always in the **cis** configuration: both hydrocarbon substituents on the same side of the double bond. Cis double bonds introduce a rigid kink of approximately 30 degrees in the otherwise linear chain. This kink prevents the chain from packing closely with neighboring chains.

**Trans** double bonds (substituents on opposite sides) do not introduce a kink. The chain remains extended and linear, like a saturated chain. Trans fatty acids in food arise primarily from industrial partial hydrogenation of vegetable oils (used to make solid margarines and processed foods) and, to a lesser extent, from microbial biohydrogenation in ruminant animals. The linear geometry of trans chains allows them to pack tightly in membranes, behaving more like saturated fats than unsaturated ones. Epidemiological and mechanistic evidence links high dietary trans fat intake to cardiovascular disease, and most regulatory bodies now severely restrict or ban industrial trans fats in food.

The physical consequence of cis vs. trans configuration is visible at room temperature. Saturated fats are solid at room temperature (lard, butter, coconut oil): their chains pack tightly and regular packing raises the melting temperature. Oils rich in cis-unsaturated fatty acids are liquid at room temperature (olive oil, vegetable oils): the kinks prevent close packing and lower the melting temperature. This directly predicts membrane behavior.

### Ionization State

The carboxylate group of a fatty acid has a $pK_a$ around 4.5 to 5, meaning that at physiological pH (~7.4) essentially all fatty acids are fully ionized as carboxylates ($-COO^-$), not protonated carboxylic acids ($-COOH$). This distinction matters for their behavior in membranes and in metabolic intermediates. The anionic carboxylate is far more water-soluble than the protonated form, but the long hydrocarbon tail still makes free fatty acids poorly soluble in water and dependent on carrier proteins (albumin in the blood, fatty acid-binding proteins intracellularly) for transport.

## Triacylglycerols: Storage Lipids

### Structure

Cells store fatty acids not as free fatty acids but as **triacylglycerols** (triglycerides, or TAG). A triacylglycerol consists of a three-carbon **glycerol** backbone with a fatty acid esterified to each of the three hydroxyl groups via an ester bond, with loss of water. The three fatty acids can be identical or different. In natural triacylglycerols, the sn-1, sn-2, and sn-3 positions (numbered on the glycerol backbone using stereospecific numbering) often carry different fatty acids, with unsaturated fatty acids tending to occupy the sn-2 position.

The ester bond is formed between the carboxylate of the fatty acid and the hydroxyl of glycerol: $R\text{-COO-}CH_2\text{-}...$. It is the same bond chemistry as glycosidic bond formation in carbohydrates: condensation with loss of water. Hydrolysis of ester bonds by lipases releases the free fatty acids and glycerol.

### Why Lipids Are Better Energy Storage Than Glycogen

Triacylglycerols store considerably more energy per gram than carbohydrates. Oxidation of fatty acids yields approximately 9 kcal/g, while carbohydrates yield approximately 4 kcal/g. This difference has two sources.

First, fatty acids are more reduced than carbohydrates. Recall that oxidation state measures the degree of reduction (or oxidation) of carbon. Carbohydrate carbons are partially oxidized already (each has an attached oxygen), while fatty acid carbons are almost fully reduced (mostly carbon-hydrogen bonds). More electrons per carbon means more energy released when those electrons are transferred to oxygen in the ETC.

Second, triacylglycerols are stored anhydrously. Glycogen, as discussed in Chapter 5, is highly hydrophilic and retains substantial water: roughly 2 to 3 grams of water per gram of glycogen. This water adds mass without adding energy. Adipose tissue stores triacylglycerols in essentially anhydrous lipid droplets. A 70 kg person with 15% body fat carries roughly 10 kg of triacylglycerol storing about 90,000 kcal of energy. The same energy stored as hydrated glycogen would require roughly 60 kg of material. Evolution solved the mobile energy storage problem with lipids, not polysaccharides.

## Phospholipids: The Bilayer Builders

### Glycerophospholipid Structure

The membrane-forming lipids in most cells are **glycerophospholipids**. Like triacylglycerols, they have a glycerol backbone and two fatty acids at sn-1 and sn-2. The difference is at sn-3: instead of a third fatty acid, there is a phosphate group esterified to the glycerol. The phosphate is in turn esterified to a small **head group** molecule, which determines the specific phospholipid class.

The general structure is:

$$\text{[Fatty acid] - Glycerol - [Fatty acid]}$$
$$\hspace{6em}|\hspace{6em}$$
$$\text{Phosphate - Head group}$$

The two fatty acid tails are the hydrophobic portion. The phosphate-head group is the hydrophilic portion. This is what makes glycerophospholipids amphipathic.

### Head Groups

The four major head groups in mammalian membranes are choline, ethanolamine, serine, and inositol. The corresponding phospholipids are:

**Phosphatidylcholine (PC)**, also called lecithin. Choline is a quaternary amine: $-O-CH_2-CH_2-N^+(CH_3)_3$. The nitrogen carries a permanent positive charge, and the phosphate is negatively charged, giving a zwitterionic (net neutral) head group. PC is the most abundant phospholipid in most mammalian membranes and is the predominant component of pulmonary surfactant.

**Phosphatidylethanolamine (PE)**. The head group is ethanolamine: $-O-CH_2-CH_2-NH_3^+$. The amine is a primary amine with a $pK_a$ near 9.5, so it is protonated and positively charged at physiological pH. PE head groups are smaller than PC head groups and pack more tightly. PE is concentrated in the inner (cytoplasmic) leaflet of the plasma membrane.

**Phosphatidylserine (PS)**. The head group is the amino acid serine. At physiological pH, the phosphate is negatively charged and the serine amine is positively charged, giving a net negative charge overall. PS is normally found almost exclusively in the inner leaflet of the plasma membrane. Externalization of PS to the outer leaflet is a signal for apoptosis (programmed cell death): phagocytes have receptors that recognize PS on the outer leaflet and use it as an "eat me" signal to engulf dying cells. The enzyme **flippase** (an ATP-dependent enzyme) maintains PS asymmetry by actively translocating PS from the outer to the inner leaflet; its failure in apoptotic cells (through caspase inactivation) allows PS to appear on the outer surface.

**Phosphatidylinositol (PI)**. Inositol is a six-carbon cyclitol with hydroxyl groups. The inositol head group can be phosphorylated at multiple positions by kinases, generating phosphatidylinositol bisphosphate ($PIP_2$) and related species that are critical second messengers in signal transduction. Phospholipase C cleaves $PIP_2$ to release inositol trisphosphate ($IP_3$, which opens calcium channels in the ER) and diacylglycerol (DAG, which activates protein kinase C). The lipid itself is the signaling precursor.

### Sphingolipids

Not all membrane lipids use a glycerol backbone. **Sphingolipids** are built on **sphingosine**, a long-chain amino alcohol:

$$HO-CH=CH-(CH_2)_{12}-CH_3 \text{ with a } -NH_2 \text{ group}$$

A single fatty acid is attached to the amino group of sphingosine via an amide bond, producing a **ceramide** as the common backbone of all sphingolipids. The distinction between sphingolipid classes comes from what is attached to the 1-hydroxyl of sphingosine:

**Sphingomyelin**: phosphocholine is attached to the 1-hydroxyl. Sphingomyelin is thus the sphingolipid analog of phosphatidylcholine: same head group, different backbone. It is abundant in the myelin sheath of nerve cells and in the outer leaflet of the plasma membrane. The enzyme **sphingomyelinase** degrades sphingomyelin in lysosomes; its deficiency causes Niemann-Pick disease, discussed in "When It Breaks."

**Cerebrosides**: a single sugar (usually glucose or galactose) is attached to the 1-hydroxyl. Cerebrosides are the simplest glycolipids and are abundant in neural tissue.

**Gangliosides**: a complex oligosaccharide containing one or more sialic acid residues is attached to the 1-hydroxyl. Gangliosides carry a net negative charge from the sialic acid. They are abundant in the outer leaflet of neuronal plasma membranes and participate in cell recognition. Ganglioside $GM_2$ is the substrate of hexosaminidase A; deficiency of this enzyme causes Tay-Sachs disease (discussed in the context of lysosomal storage diseases in other chapters).

### Cholesterol

**Cholesterol** is structurally unrelated to glycerophospholipids and sphingolipids. It is a **sterol**: four fused hydrocarbon rings (three six-membered, one five-membered) with a short branched hydrocarbon tail and a single hydroxyl group at C3. The hydroxyl is the only hydrophilic part of the molecule; the fused ring system and tail are entirely hydrophobic.

Cholesterol is found in animal cell membranes but not in plant cell membranes or bacterial membranes (plants and bacteria use other sterols or sterol-like molecules for related purposes). In mammalian cells, cholesterol constitutes up to 40% of the lipids in the plasma membrane by mole fraction. It inserts into the bilayer with its hydroxyl group near the hydrophilic headgroup region and its rigid ring system nestled between the fatty acid chains.

Cholesterol is also the precursor for steroid hormones (cortisol, testosterone, estrogen, aldosterone), bile acids, and vitamin D. These downstream functions are covered in Chapter 14.

## The Lipid Bilayer

### Self-Assembly and the Hydrophobic Effect

When amphipathic lipids are dispersed in water, they spontaneously self-assemble. The specific structure that forms depends on the geometry of the molecule. Phospholipids, which have a roughly cylindrical shape (the cross-sectional area of the two fatty acid tails roughly equals that of the head group), tend to form bilayers rather than micelles. In a bilayer, two sheets of lipids are arranged with tails facing inward, completely shielded from water, and heads facing outward, in contact with water on both sides.

The driving force is the hydrophobic effect, discussed in Chapter 2 in the context of protein folding. Water molecules adjacent to hydrophobic surfaces must adopt constrained, ordered arrangements to maintain hydrogen bonding with each other: this imposes an entropic cost. Shielding hydrophobic tails from water inside the bilayer releases those water molecules into bulk solution, increasing entropy. The process is spontaneous. No enzyme is required. No template is required. The information for membrane assembly is encoded entirely in the shape and chemistry of the lipid molecule itself.

This is a profound point for thinking about the origin of life. Lipid bilayers self-assemble from simple chemical principles. Fatty acids, which are simpler than phospholipids, also self-assemble into vesicles under certain conditions. The first protocell membranes may have required no biosynthesis at all, just the appropriate chemistry and a supply of amphipathic molecules.

### The Fluid Mosaic Model

The current model of membrane structure is the **fluid mosaic model**, proposed by Singer and Nicolson in 1972. The key features:

1. The lipid bilayer is a two-dimensional fluid, not a solid. Lipid molecules can diffuse laterally within their leaflet rapidly (diffusion constants on the order of $10^{-8}$ cm$^2$/s). Flip-flop (movement between leaflets) is extremely slow in the absence of enzymes because the hydrophilic head group must pass through the hydrophobic core.

2. Membrane proteins are embedded in this fluid lipid matrix. Some proteins span the entire bilayer (**transmembrane proteins**), some are anchored to one leaflet by a single hydrophobic helix or a lipid modification, and some are peripherally associated through electrostatic or other non-covalent interactions.

3. The membrane is asymmetric. The outer and inner leaflets differ in lipid composition, and membrane proteins have defined orientations. This asymmetry is established during biosynthesis and maintained actively.

### Membrane Fluidity and Its Regulation

Membrane fluidity describes how easily lipid molecules can move past each other within the plane of the bilayer. Fluidity is not simply a temperature-dependent property; cells actively regulate the lipid composition of their membranes to maintain appropriate fluidity across a range of temperatures.

**Chain length**: longer fatty acid chains have more van der Waals contacts with neighbors and pack more tightly, decreasing fluidity.

**Degree of unsaturation**: each cis double bond introduces a kink that prevents close packing of neighboring chains, decreasing the number of van der Waals contacts and increasing fluidity. This is why membranes enriched in unsaturated fatty acids remain fluid at lower temperatures.

**Cholesterol**: cholesterol modulates fluidity in a temperature-dependent manner. At physiological temperatures, the rigid ring system of cholesterol inserts between fatty acid chains and reduces their freedom of lateral motion, decreasing fluidity from what it would be without cholesterol (so cholesterol stiffens the membrane above the phase transition temperature). At lower temperatures, cholesterol prevents the hydrocarbon chains from crystallizing into a gel phase, maintaining some fluidity below what would otherwise be the phase transition temperature. In both cases, cholesterol buffers the membrane against extremes of fluidity: the biological phrase is that cholesterol is a "fluidity buffer."

**Temperature adaptation**: bacteria and cold-blooded animals adjust membrane unsaturation in response to temperature. Bacteria grown at lower temperatures incorporate more unsaturated fatty acids into their membranes, maintaining appropriate fluidity. Humans maintain a narrow core temperature and do not need to do this, but the same principle governs why cold-water fish (like salmon, rich in polyunsaturated omega-3 fatty acids) have highly fluid membranes even near 0$^\circ$C.

### Lipid Rafts

Not all regions of the membrane are equivalent. Cholesterol and sphingolipids tend to associate with each other in ordered, tightly packed microdomains called **lipid rafts**, which are less fluid than the surrounding membrane. These rafts are thought to serve as platforms for organizing specific signaling proteins and receptors. GPI-anchored proteins (proteins attached to the outer leaflet via a glycophosphatidylinositol lipid anchor) tend to concentrate in lipid rafts. The detailed biology of lipid rafts remains an active area of research, and their properties in live cells are difficult to study precisely. For this course, the key point is that membrane fluidity is not uniform: the lipid bilayer has lateral heterogeneity that contributes to membrane function.

### Membrane Asymmetry

The two leaflets of the plasma membrane have distinct lipid compositions, and this asymmetry is maintained by active mechanisms.

**Outer (extracellular) leaflet**: enriched in phosphatidylcholine, sphingomyelin, and glycolipids. All glycolipids face outward, contributing to the glycocalyx.

**Inner (cytoplasmic) leaflet**: enriched in phosphatidylethanolamine and phosphatidylserine. PI and $PIP_2$ are also concentrated in the inner leaflet, accessible to cytoplasmic kinases, phospholipases, and signaling proteins.

This asymmetry is established in the ER during membrane biosynthesis and maintained by:
- **Flippases**: ATP-dependent enzymes that specifically translocate PS and PE from outer to inner leaflet.
- **Floppases**: ATP-dependent transporters that move lipids from inner to outer leaflet.
- **Scramblases**: bidirectional transporters activated by calcium; activated in apoptosis and platelet activation, randomizing the bilayer and allowing PS to appear on the outer leaflet.

The exposure of PS on the outer leaflet during apoptosis is a well-characterized signal for phagocytosis. In platelets, PS externalization occurs upon activation and is necessary for the assembly of the prothrombinase complex on the platelet surface, a key step in blood coagulation.

## Pulmonary Surfactant: A Lipid at Work

The alveolus faces a physical problem that lipids solve. The alveolus is a small, fluid-lined sphere approximately 200 to 300 micrometers in diameter. The air-liquid interface at the alveolar surface has a surface tension because water molecules at the surface experience unbalanced inward attractions and resist expansion of the surface area.

The physical relationship between surface tension, pressure, and sphere radius is given by the **Law of Laplace** for a sphere:

$$\Delta P = \frac{2\gamma}{r}$$

where $\Delta P$ is the pressure difference across the wall, $\gamma$ is the surface tension, and $r$ is the radius. The smaller the radius, the higher the pressure needed to keep the sphere inflated. This means that smaller alveoli, if they all had the same surface tension, would have higher internal pressure and would collapse into larger neighboring alveoli. Uniform surface tension throughout the alveolar network would cause the lungs to collapse with each breath.

**Pulmonary surfactant** solves this problem by drastically reducing surface tension at the alveolar air-liquid interface. Surfactant is a mixture secreted by **type II pneumocytes** (alveolar type II epithelial cells) into the alveolar space. Its major lipid component is **dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine (DPPC)**: phosphatidylcholine with palmitoyl (16:0) chains at both sn-1 and sn-2. Both chains are fully saturated, making the molecule relatively rigid and allowing it to pack tightly at the air-liquid interface, forming a compressed monolayer that reduces surface tension from approximately 70 mN/m (pure water) to nearly 0 mN/m when the surfactant film is compressed during exhalation.

Surfactant production begins at approximately 24 to 28 weeks of gestation and increases significantly through the third trimester. Premature birth before surfactant is adequate means the neonate must breathe with high surface tension in the alveoli. The result is progressive alveolar collapse with each exhalation: the alveoli cannot be re-inflated without very high distending pressure, leading to **respiratory distress syndrome (RDS)** of the newborn, formerly called hyaline membrane disease. The "ground glass" appearance on chest X-ray reflects diffuse microatelectasis.

Treatment is intratracheal administration of exogenous surfactant (prepared from bovine or porcine lungs, or synthetic). Delivering DPPC and the associated surfactant proteins directly to the alveolar space immediately reduces surface tension, allowing the alveoli to stay open. The clinical response, as seen in the opener, can be dramatic and rapid because the problem is purely mechanical: the missing component is a lipid, and providing it restores normal alveolar physics.

## When It Breaks

### Respiratory Distress Syndrome

Described in detail in the surfactant section above. RDS is the direct consequence of insufficient DPPC at the alveolar surface. Antenatal corticosteroids (given to the mother if premature birth is anticipated) accelerate fetal lung maturation and increase surfactant production, reducing the incidence and severity of RDS. This is one of the most evidence-based interventions in neonatal medicine and its mechanism is straightforward: glucocorticoids accelerate type II pneumocyte differentiation and increase the synthesis of DPPC and surfactant proteins.

### Familial Hypercholesterolemia

**Familial hypercholesterolemia (FH)** is an autosomal dominant disorder caused by loss-of-function mutations in the **LDL receptor** gene. It is one of the most common serious Mendelian disorders, affecting approximately 1 in 250 people in its heterozygous form. Homozygous FH, affecting approximately 1 in 1,000,000, causes severe disease in childhood.

To understand FH, you need to know how cholesterol is transported in the blood and how cells regulate their cholesterol uptake.

Cholesterol is hydrophobic and insoluble in plasma. It is transported in lipoproteins: complexes of lipid and protein where the protein components (apolipoproteins) are amphipathic and coat the hydrophobic lipid core. Low-density lipoprotein (LDL) is the primary carrier of cholesterol from the liver to peripheral tissues. LDL is a spherical particle containing approximately 1,500 molecules of cholesterol esters in its core, surrounded by a phospholipid monolayer and a single molecule of apolipoprotein B-100 (apoB).

Cells take up LDL by **receptor-mediated endocytosis**: the LDL receptor on the cell surface binds apoB on LDL, the receptor-LDL complex is internalized in a clathrin-coated vesicle, the vesicle fuses with a lysosome, and lysosomal acid lipase hydrolyzes the cholesterol esters to release free cholesterol. This cholesterol then:

- Suppresses cholesterol synthesis by inhibiting HMG-CoA reductase (the rate-limiting enzyme in the cholesterol biosynthesis pathway).
- Suppresses LDL receptor synthesis by reducing transcription through the SREBP pathway.
- Is esterified and stored in lipid droplets if excess.

This is a feedback loop: when intracellular cholesterol rises, cells stop making more and stop taking in more from LDL. When intracellular cholesterol falls, cells make more LDL receptors and take up more.

In FH, the LDL receptor is absent or dysfunctional. LDL cannot be taken up efficiently. LDL accumulates in the plasma because the normal clearance mechanism is absent. In heterozygotes (one normal, one mutant allele), LDL receptors are reduced by roughly half and LDL is elevated to 2 to 3 times normal. In homozygotes, LDL receptors are essentially absent and LDL can reach 8 to 10 times normal.

The excess LDL in the plasma deposits cholesterol in the walls of arteries (atherosclerosis), in tendons (xanthomas), and in the skin around the eyes (xanthelasmas). The mechanism of arterial disease is complex, but the initiating event is oxidation of LDL in the arterial wall, leading to uptake by macrophages, foam cell formation, and a chronic inflammatory process that builds fibrous plaques. In homozygous FH, coronary disease and myocardial infarction can occur in childhood; the 10-year-old in the opener is a classic homozygous FH presentation.

The connection to lipid biology is direct: the cell's mechanism for regulating cholesterol homeostasis operates through a membrane receptor whose function depends on internalization and lysosomal processing. When that receptor fails, the feedback loop is broken and plasma cholesterol rises unchecked. Treatment with **statins** (inhibitors of HMG-CoA reductase, the rate-limiting enzyme in cholesterol synthesis) works primarily by reducing intracellular cholesterol, which upregulates LDL receptor expression in the liver, increasing LDL clearance from plasma. In FH homozygotes with no functional receptors, statins are largely ineffective.

### Niemann-Pick Disease

**Niemann-Pick disease** comprises several distinct disorders; the most common relevant to membrane biochemistry are types A and B, caused by deficiency of **acid sphingomyelinase**, the lysosomal enzyme that degrades sphingomyelin to ceramide and phosphocholine.

When sphingomyelinase is absent or deficient, sphingomyelin accumulates in lysosomes throughout the body, particularly in cells of the reticuloendothelial system (macrophages, liver, spleen). The lysosomes enlarge and eventually disrupt cell function.

Type A (severe infantile form) causes progressive neurodegeneration and death in early childhood. Type B (attenuated form) causes organomegaly and pulmonary disease but is compatible with survival into adulthood with variable neurological involvement. The mechanism is straightforward: sphingomyelin turnover is a normal part of membrane biology; when the lysosomal degradation step fails, the accumulated lipid cannot be recycled.

A related condition, Niemann-Pick type C, involves a different defect: the NPC1/NPC2 proteins that transport cholesterol out of the lysosome. This is a separate disease from types A and B (different gene, different mechanism) but illustrates the same principle: the cell has intracellular machinery for processing membrane-derived lipids in the lysosome, and failure of any step in that machinery causes a lysosomal storage disorder.

### Trans Fats and Cardiovascular Disease

Industrial trans fatty acids, produced by partial hydrogenation of vegetable oils, increase plasma LDL and decrease plasma HDL (high-density lipoprotein) simultaneously, a combination that is particularly atherogenic. The mechanism is not fully characterized but involves effects on cholesterol ester transfer protein and hepatic lipase. Because trans chains pack tightly in membranes (behaving like saturated fatty acids), they also alter membrane fluidity and may affect the function of membrane-embedded enzymes and receptors. The FDA effectively banned partially hydrogenated oils from the US food supply in 2018. The displacement of trans fats in processed foods is associated with measurable reductions in cardiovascular events in populations where the change was implemented.

## The MCAT Angle

### What Gets Tested

Lipid MCAT questions cluster around membrane structure, amphipathicity, fluidity, and transport.

**Amphipathicity and self-assembly**: Know that phospholipids are amphipathic and why they form bilayers rather than micelles (cylindrical geometry, two tails). Know the distinction between phospholipids (bilayer-forming, amphipathic) and triacylglycerols (not amphipathic, storage fat). A question presenting a novel amphipathic molecule and asking what structure it would form in water is testing this principle.

**Membrane fluidity**: The MCAT tests the factors that increase or decrease fluidity. More unsaturated fatty acids = more fluid. Longer chains = less fluid. More cholesterol = less fluid at body temperature (but prevents solidification at low temperature). Identify the membrane composition that would be most/least fluid; rank a set of fatty acids for predicted membrane properties.

**Cholesterol's dual role**: Cholesterol is a membrane component and a biosynthetic precursor. MCAT questions sometimes test whether students know that statins work by reducing cholesterol synthesis (by inhibiting HMG-CoA reductase), which upregulates LDL receptors, which increases LDL clearance. The mechanism of statin action requires knowing the cellular feedback loop described in the FH section.

**Passive vs. active membrane transport**: covered more fully in Chapter 7. For lipid questions, know that small nonpolar molecules (O$_2$, CO$_2$, steroid hormones) cross membranes by simple diffusion; charged molecules and large polar molecules cannot. Lipid bilayers are selectively permeable.

**Surfactant**: A question presenting a scenario of premature birth and respiratory failure may test whether you know that surfactant's mechanism involves reducing surface tension (not providing oxygen, not preventing infection, not expanding the lungs). Laplace's law ($\Delta P = 2\gamma/r$) is testable: a smaller sphere requires greater pressure difference to remain inflated; reducing $\gamma$ reduces the required pressure.

### The Reasoning Pattern

Lipid questions on the MCAT often require connecting molecular structure to physical properties to clinical outcome. The chain of reasoning for membrane fluidity questions is: composition change $\to$ packing change $\to$ fluidity change $\to$ physiological consequence. For transport questions: chemical properties of the molecule $\to$ whether it can cross the bilayer passively $\to$ what mechanism is required if it cannot. Practice moving through this chain quickly in either direction: from molecular detail to clinical outcome, and from clinical outcome backward to the molecular defect.

## Worked Problems

### Problem 1

A researcher studying membrane physiology compares two artificial bilayer vesicles. Vesicle A contains only dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine (DPPC, with two 16:0 palmitoyl chains). Vesicle B contains only dioleoylphosphatidylcholine (DOPC, with two 18:1$\Delta9$ oleoyl chains in the cis configuration). Both vesicles are incubated at 22$^\circ$C.

1. Which vesicle has a more fluid membrane at 22$^\circ$C?
2. Cholesterol is added to both vesicles in a 1:1 molar ratio with phospholipid. Predict how the fluidity of each changes.
3. A small, uncharged drug molecule is being tested for its ability to cross the membrane by simple diffusion. For which vesicle would you expect faster passive diffusion of this drug?

**Reasoning:**

1. DPPC has two saturated (16:0) chains. Saturated chains have no kinks and pack closely together, maximizing van der Waals contacts. The melting temperature of DPPC bilayers is approximately 41$^\circ$C; at 22$^\circ$C, a DPPC bilayer is in the gel phase (relatively rigid and ordered). DOPC has two cis-monounsaturated (18:1) chains. Each cis double bond introduces a kink, preventing close packing. DOPC bilayers have a melting temperature around $-20^\circ$C; at 22$^\circ$C, DOPC is well above its melting temperature and in the fluid (liquid crystalline) phase. Vesicle B (DOPC) has a more fluid membrane at 22$^\circ$C.

2. Cholesterol inserts between lipid chains with its rigid ring system. For Vesicle A (DPPC, gel phase at 22$^\circ$C): cholesterol disrupts the tight packing of saturated chains and increases fluidity (raises the gel-to-fluid transition). For Vesicle B (DOPC, fluid phase at 22$^\circ$C): cholesterol reduces the already-high lateral mobility of the unsaturated chains, decreasing fluidity. In both cases, cholesterol moves the membrane toward an intermediate, physiologically optimal range.

3. Passive diffusion of a small uncharged drug across a lipid bilayer is faster through a more fluid membrane, where the lipid chains are disordered and the drug can dissolve into and diffuse through the hydrophobic core more easily. At 22$^\circ$C, Vesicle B (DOPC, fluid phase) would show faster passive diffusion.

**Answer:** Vesicle B is more fluid at 22$^\circ$C. Cholesterol increases fluidity in Vesicle A (gel phase) and decreases it in Vesicle B (fluid phase). Passive diffusion is faster through Vesicle B.

### Problem 2

A newborn infant born at 34 weeks gestation develops increasing respiratory distress over the first 6 hours of life. The clinical team administers betamethasone to the mother 48 hours before delivery; at delivery, the infant receives intratracheal surfactant and is placed on a ventilator with continuous positive airway pressure.

1. Why is CPAP (continuous positive airway pressure) used rather than a standard ventilator that allows pressure to drop to zero between breaths?
2. Why does the antenatal betamethasone reduce the severity of RDS even though it is given to the mother, not the infant?
3. The surfactant administered is inactivated by plasma proteins that leak into the alveoli through damaged epithelium. Why would this worsen the already-existing surfactant deficiency?

**Reasoning:**

1. CPAP maintains positive pressure in the airways even between breaths, preventing the alveoli from collapsing to zero volume at the end of exhalation. If the surfactant film is inadequate and surface tension is high, the alveoli will collapse at end-expiration when airway pressure is zero. Reopening collapsed alveoli requires more pressure than maintaining them open: the Laplace relationship shows that inflation of a very small sphere (nearly collapsed alveolus) requires very high distending pressure. CPAP prevents collapse, reducing the work of breathing and preventing the cycle of repeated collapse and reopening that causes lung injury.

2. Glucocorticoids (betamethasone is a synthetic glucocorticoid) cross the placenta and act on fetal type II pneumocytes, accelerating their maturation. Type II pneumocytes are the cells that synthesize and secrete surfactant. By stimulating the glucocorticoid receptor in fetal lung cells, betamethasone upregulates the production of DPPC and the surfactant proteins (SP-A, SP-B, SP-C, SP-D) that help organize the surfactant monolayer. The effect takes 24 to 48 hours and is most complete if delivery occurs more than 24 hours after administration.

3. Plasma proteins, particularly albumin and fibrinogen, can adsorb to the air-liquid interface of the alveolus and compete with phospholipid surfactant for surface area. When protein occupies the interface, it cannot be displaced by phospholipid and the surface tension-lowering property of DPPC is lost. This phenomenon, surfactant inactivation by protein, is particularly relevant in conditions like acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) where alveolar capillary permeability is increased and plasma proteins flood the alveolar space. The clinical implication is that exogenous surfactant may be less effective in ARDS than in pure RDS, because the underlying leak delivers inactivating proteins continuously.

**Answer:** CPAP prevents end-expiratory alveolar collapse by maintaining positive airway pressure; betamethasone accelerates fetal type II pneumocyte maturation and DPPC synthesis; plasma proteins inactivate surfactant by competing for the air-liquid interface, displacing DPPC and restoring high surface tension.

### Problem 3

A 45-year-old woman has a total cholesterol of 340 mg/dL and an LDL of 270 mg/dL. She is started on a high-intensity statin. Six weeks later her LDL is 140 mg/dL, a reduction of about 48%.

1. What is the direct biochemical target of a statin, and what does inhibiting it do to intracellular cholesterol in hepatocytes?
2. How does the change in intracellular cholesterol lead to a reduction in plasma LDL?
3. A genetic test reveals she is heterozygous for a frameshift mutation in the LDL receptor gene. Would you expect statins to work as well in a homozygous FH patient? Explain.

**Reasoning:**

1. Statins inhibit **HMG-CoA reductase**, the enzyme that catalyzes the rate-limiting step in cholesterol biosynthesis (conversion of HMG-CoA to mevalonate). When hepatocytes cannot make as much cholesterol, intracellular cholesterol concentration falls.

2. Hepatocytes sense intracellular cholesterol levels through the **SREBP (sterol regulatory element-binding protein)** pathway. When cholesterol is low, SREBP is cleaved and activated, translocating to the nucleus and upregulating transcription of the LDL receptor gene among others. More LDL receptors are expressed on the hepatocyte surface, increasing the rate at which LDL is cleared from plasma by receptor-mediated endocytosis. The reduction in plasma LDL is primarily a consequence of increased hepatic LDL uptake, not of reduced cholesterol secretion. This is why statins reduce LDL even though the liver is not the primary site of LDL production; the liver is the primary site of LDL clearance.

3. In a homozygous FH patient, both alleles of the LDL receptor gene are nonfunctional. Even if SREBP is maximally activated by statin-induced intracellular cholesterol reduction, there are no functional LDL receptors to be upregulated. The mechanism by which statins lower LDL depends on functional LDL receptors. Heterozygotes (like this patient) have one functional allele; statins work by upregulating expression from that allele, roughly doubling the number of functional receptors and achieving meaningful LDL reduction. Homozygous FH patients with zero functional receptors see little benefit from statin monotherapy. These patients require alternative approaches such as LDL apheresis (mechanical removal of LDL from plasma) or newer agents like PCSK9 inhibitors (which reduce LDL receptor degradation) or lomitapide (which inhibits hepatic VLDL secretion), though even these have limited efficacy in true LDL receptor-null homozygotes.

**Answer:** Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, reducing intracellular cholesterol in hepatocytes, which activates SREBP and upregulates LDL receptor expression, increasing hepatic LDL clearance from plasma. In homozygous FH (no functional LDL receptors), this mechanism fails and statins are largely ineffective.

## Summary

Lipids are defined by their hydrophobicity, but their biological importance arises from the subset that is amphipathic. Fatty acids, the structural units of most complex lipids, are hydrocarbon chains with a carboxylate at one end; their physical properties depend critically on chain length and on the number and geometry of double bonds, with cis double bonds reducing melting temperature by introducing kinks that prevent close chain packing.

Glycerophospholipids and sphingolipids are the major bilayer-forming lipids. Their amphipathic geometry drives spontaneous self-assembly into bilayers in aqueous solution. The identity of the head group (choline, ethanolamine, serine, inositol, or sugars) determines the charge, curvature preference, and signaling roles of different lipid classes. The lipid bilayer is not a static barrier but a regulated, asymmetric, laterally heterogeneous structure whose physical properties are tuned by lipid composition, cholesterol content, and temperature.

Cholesterol is the key modulator of membrane fluidity in animal cells. Its rigid ring system interacts with fatty acid chains to buffer against extremes of fluidity: stiffening the membrane at high temperature and preventing crystallization at low temperature. Beyond its membrane role, cholesterol is the biosynthetic precursor for steroid hormones, bile acids, and vitamin D, and its plasma levels are regulated by a feedback loop centered on the LDL receptor and the SREBP transcription factor.

The two clinical anchors of this chapter illustrate different modes of lipid dysfunction. Surfactant deficiency in premature infants is a direct consequence of the absence of a specific lipid (DPPC) that reduces alveolar surface tension; providing that lipid exogenously restores normal lung mechanics. Familial hypercholesterolemia is a failure of the cellular machinery for regulating cholesterol uptake; without functional LDL receptors, the feedback loop that controls plasma cholesterol is broken and atherosclerosis begins in childhood. In both cases, the clinical outcome follows directly from the biochemical mechanism, which is the point of studying lipid biochemistry in a clinical setting.
